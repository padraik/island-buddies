## BAXTER'S MONDAY PITCH — FULL SCENE

*Patrick's desk. Monday morning, June 1, 1996. Hot already, even at nine-thirty. The window unit in Patrick's room rattles like it's thinking about quitting. Baxter's been outside for three minutes, thinking about how to start.*

*He comes in without knocking. Sets the binder on the desk.*

*Tap. Tap.*

---

"I've been doing some reading."

*He doesn't sit.*

"Before you say anything. I know what you're going to say. You're going to say we shouldn't risk our birthday money on stocks. You're going to say we don't know enough yet. You might say we should wait for something safer.

I want you to hear me out for exactly four minutes. Then you can say anything you want."

*He opens to Tab 1.*

"Nike. You know Nike. You've worn them since third grade. The stock is at forty-six dollars. Two years ago it was eighty. Four years ago it was one-fifty. It's at its lowest price since 2014. The company is not dying. The CEO is new — a guy named Elliott Hill who came in at the end of 2024 and said the previous team lost the plot. Too much online, not enough real sports. He's fixing it.

Running shoes are up twenty percent two quarters in a row. North America sales are up nine percent. Their biggest retail partner — Dick's Sporting Goods, the one on Academy — just reported that people are actually buying athletic gear at a higher rate than a year ago. The recovery isn't hypothetical. It's in the numbers."

*Tab 2.*

"Here's what I spent the weekend figuring out. Goldman Sachs — one of the most powerful banks in the world — downgraded Nike in April. They said the turnaround is taking too long. They cut their target to fifty-two dollars. JP Morgan did the same thing. Both of them are at fifty-two dollars.

The stock is at forty-six.

So the two most cautious, pessimistic major banks on Wall Street think this stock is worth fifty-two dollars. The option I want to buy makes money if the stock goes above forty-nine dollars. We're not betting on Nike returning to greatness. We're betting it reaches a price that even the most skeptical people on the Street think it should already be at."

*He turns a page.*

"June 30th, they report their fourth quarter and their full year. Management has already told us Q4 is bad — revenue down four percent, China down twenty. That news was delivered March 31st. The stock fell fifteen percent when they said it. The market has already processed that information.

What June 30th answers is a different question: what does next year look like?

And here's what changes on June 30th. The World Cup started June 11th. It's right here — in the United States, in Mexico, in Canada. Nike makes the jerseys for six of the top ten teams. France. England. Brazil. The Netherlands. The United States. They've spent almost five billion dollars on marketing this year — the most they've ever spent. On June 30th, Elliott Hill will have nineteen days of actual real-time World Cup sales data he can reference on that call. Not projections. Actual data.

He's not just talking about last year's problems anymore. He's talking about what's happening right now, at the biggest sporting event in the world."

*Tab 3. The options page.*

"Here's the play. I want to buy one call option. Strike price forty-nine dollars. Expiring July 18th.

That's seventeen days after the earnings call on June 30th. It gives us time for the move to play out without bleeding too much in time value.

Cost: approximately one dollar and ten cents per contract. One contract controls a hundred shares. So our total cost is one hundred and ten dollars. We keep ninety in reserve. Always.

If the stock goes to fifty-two — Goldman's own price target — our option is worth about three dollars. We turn a hundred and ten into three hundred. That's two-point-seven times our money.

If Hill gets on that call and gives us an Under Armour moment — 'the hard part is behind us, here's what next year looks like' — and the stock goes to fifty-four, the option is worth five dollars. We turn a hundred and ten into five hundred. The island math starts moving."

*He looks at Patrick.*

"I looked at two other plays. A homebuilder called KB Home — I won't bore you with why it doesn't work, but the short version is the last time they beat earnings the stock still fell five percent because the guidance was cautious. Same problem. And a company called Winnebago that makes RVs — cheap options, but the math on the expected value comes out negative. I ran the numbers three different ways and it loses on average.

Nike is the one.

The risk: if management is cautious again on June 30th — if they don't give us any positive signal about next year — the stock could fall eight to twelve percent and we lose the hundred and ten. The ninety stays safe. We try again.

That's the deal."

*He closes the binder.*

"You want the island. I want the island. This is how we start."

*He sits down. First time all morning.*

## PATRICK'S DECISION FRAMEWORK

If Patrick wants help deciding, Baxter answers these questions:

**"Is this gambling?"**
*"No. Gambling is random. This is a bet with a thesis — a specific reason the stock should move, at a specific time, with a defined maximum loss. The thesis can be wrong. But it's not random."*

**"What if we lose it all?"**
*"We lose a hundred and ten dollars. We have ninety dollars left. We try again with a smaller position. One loss doesn't end the island. The island ends if we lose everything in one bet. We're not doing that — that's what the Iron Rule is for."*

**"Why not wait for something safer?"**
*"There is no safer version that makes enough money to matter. The cheapest options are on the weakest theses. The best thesis has the most uncertainty. That's options. If you want certainty, we put the money in T-bills and earn five percent a year. That's a hundred and ten dollars in ten years. The island costs five million."*

**"Should we do more than one play at once?"**
*"Not this time. First play. No exceptions to the Iron Rules until we have a track record. If Plan A wins, we immediately research what comes next and deploy the full fund into Play 2. That's how you compound."*
