# THE FOUR BAXTERS — DKNG DECISION MEETING
*Monday night, June 1. Baxter's room. The binder is open to a blank page.*
*Patrick hung up twenty minutes ago. All four are present.*

---

## OPENING — BAXTER-PRIME

**BAXTER-PRIME** set the printed options chains on the desk. Both of them. NKE first, then DKNG. He didn't start with the analysis. He started with the mistake.

"I need to say this before we look at anything. I built the entire NKE pitch around a forty-nine dollar strike. I modeled the pricing, the expected value, the Iron Rule math, the breakeven — all of it — on a strike that doesn't exist. Nike options trade in two-fifty increments. I didn't check. I presented Patrick with four-out-of-five conviction on a trade that wasn't available to buy."

Silence.

"That's on me. Not the thesis — the thesis held. But a thesis built on a nonexistent instrument isn't a trade. It's a story."

**BEARXTER**: "What do you want us to do with that."

"I want us to use it. We're about to look at a DKNG chain and make a recommendation. I want every one of us to have that mistake in our head when we do it. We check everything. We don't model instruments we haven't verified exist. And we don't tell Patrick we have something until we've seen the actual chain."

He slid the DKNG August 21 printout to the center of the desk.

"Now. Let's look."

---

## THE CHAIN — REAL NUMBERS

*DKNG: $24.67. August 21 expiration (81 days). Earnings: July 31.*

| Strike | Ask | At Risk | Iron Rule | Break-even | Needs |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| $32.5C | $0.55 | $55 | ✅ 27.5% | $33.03 | +33.9% |
| **$30C** | **$0.95** | **$95** | **✅ 47.5%** | **$30.91** | **+25.3%** |
| $27.5C | $1.64 | $164 | ❌ 82% | $29.06 | +17.8% |
| $25C | $2.53 | $253 | ❌ 126% | $27.49 | +11.4% |

**The candidate: $30C August 21 at $0.95.**
- $95 at risk. Iron Rule compliant.
- Option expires 21 days after July 31 earnings.
- Analyst consensus: $37.52 → option worth **$7.52 → 692% return**
- Morgan Stanley target: $52 → option worth **$22 → 2,215% return**

---

## THE DEBATE

**BULLXTER** picked up the chain before anyone else could speak.

"This is what we were looking for all weekend. Forty-seven-fifty percent of the fund, Iron Rule compliant, three weeks post-earnings for the move to play out. And if the stock hits analyst consensus — not the bull case, the *consensus* — we turn ninety-five dollars into seven hundred and fifty."

**BEARXTER**: "I want Calxter to run the probability before anyone gets excited."

**CALXTER** already had his pad out.

"The options market is pricing in an expected move of plus-or-minus thirteen-point-three-seven percent for earnings. The break-even on the thirty-call requires a twenty-five-point-three percent move from today's price. We need almost double the expected earnings reaction just to break even."

*He looked up.*

"So we are explicitly betting that DraftKings has an above-average earnings reaction. That is the bet. Not 'will they beat' — they might beat and we still lose. We need them to beat *and* the stock to move further than the market currently expects."

**BULLXTER**: "The market has been pricing DKNG wrong for six months. The stock is at twenty-four dollars with analysts at thirty-seven. The 'expected move' is a backward-looking calculation based on a stock that's been in a downtrend. If the downtrend reverses—"

**BEARXTER**: "If. That's doing a lot of work in that sentence."

**BULLXTER**: "If the World Cup data comes in strong and Robins gives clear Q3 guidance, the market re-rates fast. That's not plus-thirteen. That's plus-thirty, plus-forty. Look at the Under Armour comparable — twenty-four percent. Foot Locker — thirty percent. This is the same structure."

**BEARXTER**: "Under Armour and Foot Locker didn't have three consecutive EPS misses. DraftKings has missed adjusted EPS estimates three quarters in a row. The market is *not* primed to reward this company on earnings day. It's primed to punish the next miss."

**PRIME**: "Calxter. The full probability model."

**CALXTER** wrote for thirty seconds. Then read aloud.

---

## CALXTER'S PROBABILITY MODEL

"I'm modeling five scenarios for DKNG stock price on August 21st, 81 days from now. This accounts for pre-earnings drift, the earnings day move, and 21 days of post-earnings movement."

| Scenario | Prob | Driver | Stock Aug 21 | Option Value | Net |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Full thesis: World Cup blowout + raise | 15% | Handle crushes $1.1B est, prediction markets traction, Q3 guidance strong | $37–42 | $7–12 | **+$6 avg** |
| Good beat: World Cup solid, Q3 clear | 20% | Beat on handle, positive Q3 visibility | $31–36 | $1–6 | **+$2.50 avg** |
| Modest: in-line, guidance maintained | 25% | World Cup on target, no raise | $26–30 | $0 | **-$0.95** |
| Miss: weak guidance, EPS disappoints | 25% | World Cup disappoints or margins miss | $20–25 | $0 | **-$0.95** |
| Bear: guidance cut, stock breaks lower | 15% | Third consecutive miss + cut | $17–21 | $0 | **-$0.95** |

**Expected value calculation:**

EV = (0.15 × $6.00) + (0.20 × $2.50) + (0.25 × -$0.95) + (0.25 × -$0.95) + (0.15 × -$0.95)

EV = $0.90 + $0.50 - $0.238 - $0.238 - $0.143

**EV = +$0.78 on $0.95 invested = +82% expected return**

"Probability of any profit: **35%**. Probability of losing the full $95: **65%**."

*He set the pad down.*

"This is a negative-win-rate, positive-EV bet. We lose money 65% of the time. We make enough money in the winning 35% that the average outcome is strongly positive. This is not a comfortable trade to hold. It will feel like it's failing most of the time. The math says enter anyway."

---

## THE REAL ARGUMENT

**BEARXTER** stood up. Not to leave — he stayed at the desk.

"I want to name what we're actually disagreeing about, because it's not the expected value. Calxter's math is right. The disagreement is about the probability table."

He tapped the model.

"Fifteen percent for the full thesis. Twenty percent for a good beat. That's thirty-five percent total. I think those numbers are too high given the EPS miss pattern. Three misses in a row. The guidance cut. The market is already at its most skeptical — and it's been right to be skeptical. I'd put full thesis at ten percent and good beat at fifteen percent. That changes the EV materially."

**CALXTER**: "At ten and fifteen, EV drops to approximately plus twenty-two percent. Still positive."

**BEARXTER**: "Barely. And we're holding a position that's underwater for two months on a company with a miss pattern."

**BULLXTER**: "The miss pattern was on *adjusted EPS*. Revenue has beaten every single quarter. The business is growing. The misses were on accounting adjustments and one-time items. The underlying business is not broken."

**BEARXTER**: "Tell that to the stock price."

**PRIME**: "Here's the question I want us to answer. Is the EPS miss pattern structural — meaning it reflects real deterioration — or is it a guidance management problem where management set expectations too high and has now reset them?"

Quiet.

**CALXTER**: "Q4 guidance cut to $6.7B. Q1 came in at $1.646B annual run rate of $6.6B — tracking the revised guidance. They reset the bar and they're now hitting it. The miss pattern may be over."

**BEARXTER**: "May be."

**BULLXTER**: "The hold rate went from six-point-four to seven-point-eight percent in one year. That's structural margin improvement. That's not a company missing because the business is broken. That's a company that over-promised on the bottom line while the business itself got better."

*Long pause.*

**BEARXTER** sat down again.

"I don't love this trade. I want that on the record. But I'll say this: the NKE mistake was building a pitch on a number we invented. The DKNG pitch is built on an actual chain with actual prices and an actual thesis. The $30 call exists. The August 21 expiration exists. The earnings are July 31. The World Cup is real. The analyst consensus is real."

He looked at the chain.

"Thirty-five percent probability of profit. Plus-eighty-two-percent expected value. I've been right about the miss pattern. I've been right about every skeptical question I've raised this weekend. And the math still says enter."

*Pause.*

"I'm not saying yes. I'm saying I can't say no on the numbers."

---

## TIMING — NOW OR MID-JULY?

**PRIME**: "One more question. We enter now or we wait until mid-July?"

**BULLXTER**: "Now. The World Cup starts June 11th — ten days. If DraftKings starts showing up in betting volume headlines, the stock drifts up before we can enter. At $27, the $30C costs more. At $29, it costs significantly more. The ninety-five-cent price is today."

**CALXTER**: "Theta decay on a ninety-five-cent option with 81 days to expiry runs approximately one-point-two cents per day in the first month, accelerating to two-plus cents per day after July 15th. Waiting 45 days to mid-July costs approximately $0.35-0.45 in time value — roughly a third of the option premium. However, if the stock falls to $22 before mid-July, the $30C drops to $0.60 and we enter cheaper."

**BEARXTER**: "So waiting is a bet that the stock falls before mid-July."

**CALXTER**: "Waiting is a bet that the stock doesn't rise above roughly $27 before mid-July and that the time decay cost is worth the lower entry. If the stock stays flat, waiting costs us about forty cents in theta. If the stock rises, waiting costs us the whole trade."

**PRIME**: "What does the World Cup do to DKNG's stock between now and July 31?"

**BULLXTER**: "If the first weekend of group stage betting volume exceeds expectations — and DraftKings makes any public statement about it — the stock moves. Sports media will be covering the handle numbers. The CEO will be on CNBC talking about betting volume. This stock doesn't stay at twenty-four dollars through a successful World Cup without some drift."

**BEARXTER**: "Unless the group stage betting is below expectations because casual American fans don't bet soccer the way they bet football."

**PRIME**: "Which is a real risk."

**BEARXTER**: "Which is why I'm not saying enter today. I'm saying the case for entering mid-July is stronger than the case for entering now. We lose forty cents in theta. We gain certainty about whether the World Cup catalyst is actually materializing."

**CALXTER**: "Counter-argument: if World Cup week one is strong, the stock is at $28 and the $30C is $1.40. We've paid an extra forty-five cents and reduced our expected return by thirty percent."

*Silence.*

**PRIME**: "The answer is: we tell Patrick both options with honest trade-offs. We don't pretend we know whether the stock goes up or down in the next 45 days."

---

## VERDICT

**Conviction on the thesis: 3.5/5** — unchanged. The business is real. The World Cup catalyst is real. The analyst consensus is real.

**Conviction on the instrument: confirmed.** The $30C August 21 exists at $0.95. Iron Rule compliant. We checked the chain.

**Probability of profit: 35%** (Calxter base case) / **25%** (Bearxter's revised estimates)

**Expected value: +82%** (Calxter) / **+22%** (Bearxter's revised probabilities)
Both are positive. Both beat leaving the money in SGOV.

**On timing — split decision:**

*Enter now at $0.95:*
- Captures full World Cup drift potential
- Loses theta if stock stays flat
- Risk: World Cup underperforms, stock falls, we hold a losing position for 60 days

*Enter mid-July at est. $1.30-1.40:*
- Costs ~$0.40 more in premium
- Better information about World Cup trajectory
- 21 days to earnings instead of 81
- Risk: stock runs to $28-29 and we've priced ourselves out

**What Baxter Prime tells Patrick tomorrow:**

"The trade is real. The instrument exists. The math is positive. You're looking at a 35% chance of making money and a 65% chance of losing ninety-five dollars. The expected return justifies the entry. The only question is whether you enter at ninety-five cents now or wait for World Cup signal and enter at a higher price with better information. That's your call — not mine. Your money, your timeline, your tolerance for sitting on a position that will look wrong for most of the next sixty days."

---

*Bearxter stood, finally.*

*"Check the chain. Every time. Before the pitch, not after."*

*He left.*

*Nobody argued.*
