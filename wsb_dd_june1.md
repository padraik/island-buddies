## What Actually Happened

Nike had three bad years because the previous management team made a specific set of mistakes:

1. **Over-rotated to DTC (direct-to-consumer)** — cut their wholesale partners, tried to sell everything through Nike.com and the Nike app
2. **Neglected performance products** — chased lifestyle/fashion at the expense of actual athletic gear
3. **Lost China market share** to local brands (Li-Ning, Anta) during the DTC pivot

The result: revenue declining, margins compressing, stock getting cut in half.

**The fix is already happening.** A new CEO, Elliott Hill, came in Q4 2024 and said publicly that the previous team lost the plot. He's rebuilding the wholesale relationships, refocusing on sport performance, and spending more on marketing than Nike has ever spent in a single year.

**The recovery numbers are already in the data:**
- Running shoes: +20% YoY for two consecutive quarters
- North America wholesale: +9%
- Dick's Sporting Goods (Nike's biggest retail partner) just reported Q1 with **+6% comparable store sales** — the channel is healthy

## The Three Bad Things (None of Them Permanent)

**1. The Exact Sciences Acquisition**

Abbott spent **$21 billion** to acquire Exact Sciences — the company that makes Cologuard (the colon cancer screening test, the one from the football game commercials: "no prep, no sedation") and Cancerguard (a new multi-cancer early detection blood test targeting a $60 billion market).

The market didn't like the price. EPS guidance came down $0.20/share due to dilution. Stock fell $40 on the announcement.

**The bull case on the acquisition:** Cologuard is growing at **30% annually** with no meaningful competition. Cancerguard is early-stage but targeting the largest emerging category in healthcare — multi-cancer early detection from a single blood draw. Abbott paid $21 billion for a 30%-growth business in a $60B market. The short-term dilution is real. The long-term math is different.

**2. Weak Flu Season**

Abbott makes diagnostics including flu tests. Weak flu season = fewer tests = lower revenue. This happens. The flu comes back every year. This is a temporary headwind dressed up as a structural problem.

Three bad things. Three temporary things. Forty dollars of stock price erased.
