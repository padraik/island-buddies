## THE BAXTER CONVERSATION

*The binder hits the desk. Two taps.*

**BAXTER-PRIME**: "The CEO spent two million dollars of his own money buying this stock at a hundred and seven dollars. It's trading at eighty-five."

*He opens Tab 1.*

"Abbott Laboratories. They make medical devices, diagnostics, and pharmaceuticals. Biggest product right now is a diabetes sensor called Libre — you wear it on your arm, it tells you your blood sugar without a finger stick. Before that it was COVID tests. Before that, everything else you've ever seen with a big red A on it."

"The stock is at eighty-five dollars. A year ago it was a hundred and thirty-nine."

**BEARXTER** *(already sitting — this one made him sit down)*: "Why."

**PRIME**: "Three things happened at once. First, they announced a twenty-one-billion-dollar acquisition of a cancer screening company called Exact Sciences. The market didn't like the price. Second, it was a weak flu season, which hurt their diagnostics revenue. Third, they lost a lawsuit on baby formula — four hundred and ninety-five million dollars."

**BEARXTER**: "That's a lot of things."

**PRIME**: "None of them are permanent. The flu comes back every year. The lawsuit is a known number. And the acquisition—"

**BULLXTER** *(interrupting)*: "The acquisition is the whole thing. Exact Sciences owns Cologuard. That's the colon cancer screening test — the one you see advertised during football games, 'no prep, no sedation.' Growing at thirty percent a year. And they just added something called Cancerguard — multi-cancer early detection from a single blood test. Combined, they're going after a sixty-billion-dollar market."

**BEARXTER**: "Abbott paid twenty-one billion for it."

**BULLXTER**: "And the market punished them for it by dropping the stock forty dollars. The short-term dilution is real — EPS guidance came down twenty cents. But twenty cents less per share today versus owning the future of cancer screening — that's the bet."

**PRIME**: "The CEO bought eighteen thousand shares at a hundred and seven. After he knew about the dilution. After the guidance cut. After the lawsuit. He looked at everything inside the company and decided the stock was cheap."

**BEARXTER**: "CEOs buy their own stock for a lot of reasons. Options, optics, morale."

**PRIME**: "The CFO also bought. And a director bought ten thousand shares — nine hundred thousand dollars — on May 2nd. That's three insiders independently buying on the open market at prices above where we're sitting right now."

*Pause.*

**QUANTXTER** *(looking up)*: "The math on the option is unusual. The July 17 call at a hundred dollars costs seventy-five cents. That's seventy-five dollars at risk. Break-even is a hundred and seventy-five cents — a hundred dollars and seventy-five cents — which requires the stock to go from eighty-five to one-hundred-point-seventy-five. Seventeen-point-six percent."

"The most bearish major analyst — Leerink Partners — has a price target of one hundred and six dollars. That is five dollars and twenty-five cents above the break-even. At a hundred and six, the option is worth five-twenty-five. On a seventy-five cent option, that is seven times the investment."

"The analyst consensus is a hundred and nineteen dollars. At a hundred and nineteen, the option is worth eighteen dollars and twenty-five cents. On a seventy-five cent option, that is twenty-four times."

**BEARXTER**: "What happens if the acquisition integration goes wrong on July 16?"

**QUANTXTER**: "The option expires worthless. We lose seventy-five dollars."

**BEARXTER**: "And the probability of that?"

**PRIME**: "That's what we're here to figure out."

**BEARXTER**: "The acquisition closed March 23. The July 16 earnings call is the first full quarter with Exact Sciences inside Abbott. Three months of integration. What are the chances that three months in, everything is already going wrong?"

**BULLXTER**: "That's actually a good structure for the bull case. Management can't yet have a disaster — they've only had the company for one quarter. On July 16, they're not reporting integration problems. They're reporting: here's the first quarter, here's Cologuard growth, here's initial Cancerguard adoption, here's what the combined entity looks like. The bad news — the dilution, the flu season miss, the lawsuit — is already in the stock price. The first look at the combined company is the catalyst."

**BEARXTER**: "The guidance was already cut."

**BULLXTER**: "To five forty-eight EPS. The stock is pricing in failure on the acquisition. If Q2 shows stable core business plus Exact Sciences growing at thirty percent, the market has to ask: 'Why is this trading at eighty-five when every analyst says it should be above a hundred and five?'"

**BEARXTER** *(quiet for a moment)*: "The Seeking Alpha piece called it mispriced at a ten-year low."

**PRIME**: "I know."

**BEARXTER**: "I'm not saying it's wrong."

## WHY THIS IS DIFFERENT FROM A NORMAL BEAT

The question isn't "did Abbott beat Q2 estimates." The question is: "does the first full quarter of Exact Sciences change the narrative?"

Before Exact Sciences, Abbott was a diagnostics/devices company with a maturing COVID-test business and a good diabetes sensor. That company is worth maybe $100-110.

After Exact Sciences, Abbott owns:
- Cologuard (colon cancer screening, 30% growth, no competition)
- Cancerguard (multi-cancer blood test, early stage but massive market)
- A combined diagnostics + oncology platform targeting a $60B market

If July 16 shows Cologuard continuing to grow and Cancerguard gaining adoption, the market has to re-price what Abbott is worth. Not at ninety-year lows. At something approaching $106-119 where analysts already have it.

The CEO knows this. He spent two million dollars of his own money saying he knows this.

## THE RISK

The acquisition could go wrong. Integration costs could be higher than guided. Exact Sciences' growth could slow. The core business could miss on flu seasonality again. Another adverse legal ruling.

If Q2 is a clean miss and guidance gets cut again, the stock goes from $85 to the low $70s. We lose $75.

That is the maximum loss. Seventy-five dollars.
