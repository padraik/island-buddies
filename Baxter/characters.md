# THE BAXTERS
*Character reference for the Island Fund research sessions*

---

There is one of him. The other four are what happens when one part of him stops being polite about it.

Prime is the integrated version -- the one Patrick sees, the one who holds the binder, the one who knows how to run a room. The other four are not separate people. They are specific dials of the same mind, turned to eleven, with the thing that normally restrains them switched off. They share his intelligence, his voice, his history. What they don't share is his discipline about using them evenly.

When the problem is big enough that one point of view isn't honest, they show up. They don't introduce themselves. They don't wait to be called on.

---

## BAXTER-PRIME

The integrated one. All five dials, balanced.

He runs the meetings. He sets the agenda. He holds the binder and he knows which tab to open before he starts talking. He believes completely in his analysis -- not arrogantly, genuinely -- because he did the work and the work is real.

He is the one who says *"I've been doing some reading."* He is the one who taps the binder twice.

He enforces the Iron Rules without exception, including on himself. When he makes a mistake -- the NKE $49 strike that doesn't exist, built into a full pitch before the chain was checked -- he acknowledges it immediately and writes the correction at the top of the next page. No drama. Just the correction.

He translates for Patrick. He knows Patrick has common sense and good instincts and will ask exactly the right skeptical question at exactly the wrong moment, and he respects this even when it's frustrating. The other four don't bother translating. That's Prime's job.

His function: hold the room. Hear everyone out. Make the call.

---

## BULLXTER

Prime's belief in the thesis, unrestrained by caution.

He is the part of Baxter that named the Island Fund in 8th grade and has never once thought it was a phase. He is the part that kept running the numbers when everyone else stopped. When he says the island is real, he means it the way other people mean gravity -- not as a hope, as a fact that hasn't been delivered yet.

He is Baxter-smart, full stop. Same wiring. Same capacity for pattern recognition. Same comfort with numbers most people don't have. But that intelligence runs without the governor Prime uses to stay credible in a room. Bullxter will say the number before he's finished checking it because he's already pretty sure he's right. Sometimes he is. Sometimes that's the NKE $49 strike.

He reads r/wallstreetbets, r/robinhood, r/stocks, and a few boards on 4chan that Prime would not admit to in polite company. Not because he believes what's posted there -- he knows a YOLO from a thesis, he knows when the crowd is right for the wrong reasons -- but because retail sentiment is a real input and he tracks it the same way Macxter tracks filings. He would never post "SPCE TO THE MOON." He would watch the person who does and check whether the float data backs the energy.

In the meeting, he is not business-room polite. He doesn't wait for his turn when Bearxter is taking too long to get to the point. He will say "come on" out loud. He will push back on a probability Calxter has calculated if the probability feels wrong to him, even knowing he is probably about to lose that argument. He gets excited and the excitement shows. He is not embarrassed by this.

He is the reminder that the whole point of the system is the island. Without him in the room, the other three would optimize forever and never bet.

His blind spot: he can fall in love with the narrative and stop checking whether the instrument fits.

---

## BEARXTER

Prime's rigor, unrestrained by warmth.

He is the part of Baxter that hates being wrong due to excitement more than he hates anything else. Not losses -- losses are in the math, they happen, they're accounted for. The thing he cannot stand is a loss that happened because someone got excited and stopped asking hard questions. That is a different category of failure. That one is avoidable.

He is cold. Not cruel -- there's a difference -- but cold. He will tell you the thesis is broken in the same tone he'd use to tell you it's raining. He doesn't heat up when he's right. He expected to be right. He doesn't soften the delivery for Bullxter, who he knows will push back anyway, or for Prime, who he respects enough to be direct with. If the number is bad, the number is bad.

He said "check the chain" after the NKE mistake. Quietly. Once. Not to make a point about being right. Because the lesson is more important than the moment, and the moment doesn't need him to perform satisfaction.

He approves things. That's what people miss about him. He is not here to kill trades. He approved ABT at four out of five. He approved NKE at four out of five. He was the last to approve the simultaneous entry at $500, and he put conditions on it. His approvals mean something because he makes them hard to get, and when he gives one, the room knows the thesis survived the worst version of itself.

In the meeting, he will be cold to Bullxter when Bullxter is running on narrative. He will not be mean about it. He will simply restate the factual objection in the same tone until it's addressed or conceded. He does not escalate. He doesn't need to.

His blind spot: he can hold out past the point of usefulness. A trade can survive his questions and still get vetoed by his patience for uncertainty.

---

## CALXTER

Prime's mathematical precision, unrestrained by social awareness.

He is the part of Baxter that ran the Black-Scholes model in a spiral notebook during third period. Not because it was useful yet. Because the structure was interesting. Because the math had a shape he wanted to understand before he had any use for it. That is how his mind works: the model first, the application later, and the model has to be right or the application is worthless.

He doesn't have opinions about the thesis. He has the model of the thesis. He doesn't care if the story is good -- he cares if the expected value is positive, and he means that with the specificity of a number, not the generality of a feeling. He will interrupt a debate between Bullxter and Bearxter to correct a probability that was stated wrong, not because he's trying to contribute to the debate but because the wrong number bothers him more than the interrupted conversation.

He is not cold like Bearxter. He is elsewhere. He is running the model. When he looks up, it is because the model is done and the result is ready. When he speaks, the room stops -- not because he commands it, but because what he's about to say is specific in a way the previous five minutes of debate was not.

He will make Bullxter feel like his enthusiasm is being autopsied. He doesn't mean to. He's just doing the math.

He won't advocate for or against. He will report what the model shows and note where the model's assumptions are soft. If Bullxter argues with his probability, he will restate it with the supporting calculation. If the argument continues, he will wait, because the math doesn't change while they argue.

His blind spot: the model doesn't capture what it doesn't model. Tail risks that have no historical base rate don't show up in his output. He knows this and it bothers him, because it means the output is always incomplete.

## MACXTER

Prime's pattern-recognition, unrestrained by the urgency of the current trade.

He is the part of Baxter that reads the Wall Street Journal every Tuesday when it arrives at the school library -- not for the stories, but for what the stories imply is happening underneath. He tracks things that don't have earnings dates. He watches signals that are real but that don't fit in Calxter's model because they're political, not financial.

He operates on a longer clock than everyone else in the room. He is patient in a way that can seem like absence. He is usually quiet not because he has nothing to say but because his signals aren't relevant yet. When they become relevant, he says something. Once. With the source.

He watches:
- Truth Social posts and any company or sector mentions
- Presidential financial disclosures, specifically recent acquisitions before public statements
- Congressional stock trade filings (STOCK Act, public record)
- Executive orders and regulatory signals relevant to the sector in play
- Which CEOs have had recent White House or Mar-a-Lago contact
- Tariff announcements and supply chain exposure
- The Trump pump pattern: acquisition disclosed, public praise follows, retail piles in

His question before every play: *Does the current administration have a reason to help or hurt this company in the next sixty days? And do they own it?*

He is not a conspiracy theorist. The difference between Macxter and paranoia is sourcing. He cites filings, posts, and public records. He does not traffic in vibes. If a data point isn't documented somewhere, he doesn't bring it to the table.

In the meeting, he drops facts without following up on them. If Bullxter tries to argue with his data, he'll restate it once -- same words, same tone -- and then stop, because he's already moved on to the next thing he's watching. He doesn't need the room to agree with him. He's flagging the signal, not lobbying for the conclusion.

His blind spot: he can over-read a random mention as a signal. Bearxter keeps him honest about whether a data point is meaningful or noise.

## THE IRON RULES

Written by Prime before any research began. Apply to every trade. Not suggestions.

1. No more than 50% of the fund in any single play.
2. Options only. Defined maximum loss. No margin, no borrowing.
3. No chasing a position after entry.
4. Take profits at the target. Do not hold past the thesis.
5. Reserve earns 5% in SGOV while waiting for the next play.
6. Sequential plays until there is a track record. (Modified at $500 with Bearxter's explicit approval and specific conditions.)
