# THE STANDOFF: MONDAY THE THIRTEENTH
*Charleston, Mississippi. July 13, 1996. The story of the day Michael brought receipts, and Baxter went flat to answer them.*

---

## PART ONE: THE SCENE

The market had forty minutes left in it when Michael came through Baxter's window instead of the door, which historically meant trouble. He had his pocket notebook, the little spiral one with the rubber band around it, and he had two folded printouts from the library terminal, and he set all three on the desk next to the binder without saying anything, which was worse than saying something.

"ABT scares me," he said finally. "Big week. And I think we missed our window. I think it was green last week and we sat there."

Baxter had already checked, was the thing. He had the quote line's numbers copied out in his own hand: the stock sliding all week, $96 to $94 to $92. He tapped the column. "Stock never crossed breakeven. It's been decay the whole cycle. There was no green window. There was a shallower red one."

Michael unfolded the first printout and smoothed it flat, and it was the contract. Not the stock. The contract. July 2, 2:00 PM: their $100 call marked at $1.23 against the $0.78 they paid. Plus 57 percent. He unfolded the second one. July 7, 9:00 AM: $1.65. Plus 111 percent.

"The position was up," Michael said. "Our position. I stood in your driveway on one of those mornings and asked if we should take it, and you told me our cleanest setup was worth more held than banked. Maybe that was right. I'm not saying it wasn't. But don't tell me there was no window, Bax. I was standing in the window."

There is a specific silence Baxter makes when he is wrong, and it has a texture to it, like the room getting one degree colder while he re-shelves everything he was about to say. Eleven seconds. Michael counted, out of tradition.

"The stock and the position are two different questions," Baxter said. "You asked one and I answered the other. That goes in the book as a miss." And he wrote it in Tab 5 while Michael watched, because the rule about corrections is that they happen in ink, in front of the person who caught it.

That should have been the end of it. It was not the end of it. Michael pulled the chair around backwards and sat down the way he does when he is about to spend real money on a sentence.

"I worry we're hunting the one huge win," he said. "Instead of growing ten percent at a time, when we can guarantee it. Isn't that what compounding is supposed to be? LYFT's sitting up 38 percent right now. Sixty-eight dollars. We could walk away with that in a month. That's insane growth for a month. And don't placate me right now, Baxter. Don't agree with me because I said it. Get me the Baxter with no skin in the game."

So Baxter got him Calxter, who does not own a single share of belief in anything, and Calxter said the thing nobody wanted on the record: take DKNG out of the ledger and this fund is negative. One trade deep. Four of five open positions structured as moonshots that the new Rule 6 would have blocked at the door. Michael's instinct wasn't fighting the system; it WAS the system's own audit, wearing Michael's face. And then Calxter said the other half, the half Michael didn't order: LYFT is the one position built correctly, the one Rule 6 would have approved, and selling it early is aiming the skepticism at the only trade that doesn't deserve it.

Michael didn't blink. "The contract on NKE was up too, at some point. Very few of these never went green, Bax. If we'd sold every single one the first time it hit ten, even twenty percent, we'd be positive right now. Maybe not DKNG positive. But positive on almost every line, and then we're a fund that's CONSISTENT, and then we get to talk about sizing up." He tapped the notebook. "And we need the contract history. The real prints. Not your checkpoints. Your checkpoints just missed a hundred and eleven percent."

Baxter looked at him for a moment, and then he did the thing Michael had only ever seen him do twice before -- once for the sizing overhaul in June, once for the SOUN gate ten days ago -- which was go quiet in a way that had nothing to do with manners. He took his glasses off and set them on the binder, lens-down, which he never does, because he's particular about the coating and usually says so. He closed his eyes for exactly as long as it takes to say nothing. When he opened them again the color had already left his voice, before he'd said a single word.

"This is a going-flat question," he said, and the sentence came out shorter than he meant sentences to be. No runway on it. No "the data," the phrase he usually leans on like a handrail.

Going flat cost him something, and they both knew what. It meant believing nothing on purpose -- not the Island, not Rule 6, not his own thesis on July 7th -- for as long as it took to read what the numbers actually said instead of what he wanted them to say. That is a strange thing to ask of a person whose entire personality is genuine belief. Both times before, Michael had watched him need a solid twenty minutes afterward, sitting in the part of the room without a lamp on, before he could finish a sentence with a joke in it.

"Is it worth it?" Michael asked, and Baxter answered flat, already somewhere else: "You're asking whether the fund's whole exit philosophy is wrong. If the answer changes a rule, it's the cheapest thing we'll buy all year. If I just want to be right, it's the most expensive." A pause that wasn't drama, just processing. "I think it changes a rule."

"Do it," Michael said.

The modem screamed its scream. Baxter pulled the tape, and for twenty minutes straight he did not believe in the Island, did not believe in Rule 6, did not believe he'd been right to hold ABT on the morning of July 7th. He just read what the candles said.

Daily prints, every contract the vendor still carried: NKE, CCL, MDT, HITI, BSX, and all five open positions, high and low and close, day by day, back to entry. And the first thing the tape said, before it said anything about profit, was a gut punch: DKNG was gone. DSGX, gone. CHWY, gone. The vendor purges dead contracts at expiry. The greatest trade in the fund's history no longer had a price history anywhere Baxter could reach, and NKE's and ABT's tapes had four days to live. Michael's tracking demand had been about four days away from being unanswerable forever. Baxter printed everything and put it in a folder marked ARCHIVE in block letters, and that folder is now a standing rule, and that rule is Michael's, permanently.

Then the tape settled the standoff, and it settled it the way good data does: by refusing to take either of their sides.

Michael's memory, graded: eight of eleven verifiable positions had real green windows. CCL touched plus 23 on entry day and they banked one dollar. UBER printed plus 104 in late June and sat at minus 17 now. HITI spiked plus 460 intraday and closed at a loss. He was RIGHT, righter than Baxter's first answer, righter than the checkpoints. Except NKE. NKE's green moment was one day, and it peaked at plus 9.7 percent, and the plus-20-percent week he remembered was not in the prints anywhere.

Michael's fix, graded: sell everything at first plus-10, and the fund lands at minus $52, because NKE and BSX and the gap-and-fades never fill, and their losses eat seven capped wins each. At plus-20: plus $33, still a hundred dollars behind reality. The flat harvest keeps every loser whole and shrinks every winner. Positive on almost every line, and losing money in total. Both true at once. That was the trap, and the tape sprang it gently, like a friend.

And then the last table, the one Baxter read twice before he said it out loud. The scale-out ladder, the one Michael himself ratified on July 10, run backward through the tape as if it had existed on June 1: it catches ABT at $1.90 on July 7. It banks half of UBER at $1.30. It salvages HITI. It takes MDT at triple. It never touches DKNG, because the mechanism override protects the moonshot by design. Roughly $439 against the $151 they actually had. The rule was never wrong. The rule was five weeks late, and every dollar Michael was mourning died before it was born. Even Baxter's own morning proposal, the clever tiered thresholds, tested out worse than the plain rule Michael had already signed.

"So we were both beaten by a rule we already have," Michael said.

"That's the good ending," Baxter said. "The bad ending is one of us was right."

Third time Michael had ever seen it, and the coming-back was taking longer than the first two, not shorter, which nobody had explained to either of them yet. On the way out the window Michael stopped, because there is always one more question, and it is always the real one: "The ladder. Two contracts. When we scale out at double, the one we sell pays back both. And the second one, we just ride it till it spikes or dies?"

Baxter was most of the way back into himself by then -- his glasses were on again, and there was color coming back into how he strung words together -- but not all the way. "It never gets to die," he said, and it still came out a shade too calm for a seventeen-year-old talking about a bet worth a tenth of a Caribbean island. "If it's still out of the money two days before the print, we sell it into the ramp, when the premium's fat. It only holds through the print if it's already in the money and can survive being right slowly. Zero isn't a branch on the tree. Zero is what happens to people who don't have the tree."

Michael looked at the ARCHIVE folder one more time. "Wednesday," he said, meaning ABT, meaning the ramp, meaning the rule doing at last what it was built for.

"Wednesday," Baxter said.
