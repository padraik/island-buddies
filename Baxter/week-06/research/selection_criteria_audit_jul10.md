## FINDING 1: RULE 1 FIGHTS THE STRONGEST FORCE IN THE MARKET

Rule 1 for calls buys the bottom 20-25% of the 52-week range. The logic was dislocation: buy the market's mistake, collect the correction.

The problem is that this is a bet against momentum, and momentum is not a vibe, it is among the most persistent effects in a century of market data. Stocks near 52-week lows keep underperforming on multi-week horizons far more often than they snap back. Stocks near 52-week highs keep outperforming. Mean reversion is real but it is a 1-month, low-magnitude effect. A 6-week option needs a gap; dislocated large caps deliver a grind.

**Our own ledger says the same thing:**
- Classic bottom-quartile screen entries: NKE, CCL, CHWY, HITI, BSX, ABT, TRMB, UBER, LYFT, LVS. Realized results from that set so far: 0 wins, 6 losses/flat (CCL +$1 counted as flat). LYFT is the only green mark among the open five.
- The fund's 2 realized winners were NOT products of the bottom-quartile screen. MDT was an opportunistic post-earnings momentum entry (stock rising through a trigger, catalyst just confirmed). DKNG was a mid-range recovery with a dated sentiment mechanism. Both were entries the screen would never have generated.

For the second time in one day, the record shows the profits coming from the exceptions.

**The nuance Bearxter insisted on:** Rule 1 is not wrong about WHERE the upside room is. A stock at its low with a $65 bear floor has room; a stock at its high has none, and Rule 4 needs that gap to exist. Rule 1 is wrong about WHEN the room gets filled. The correction on a beaten-down large cap arrives in quarters, not weeks. The only bottom-quartile names that gap hard enough for a 6-week option are the volatile ones: cheap stocks, high short interest, high beta, small and mid caps. Which is exactly the population Rule 6 now admits and Rule 5 can afford.

**Resolution:** Rule 1 stands, but it is demoted from thesis to precondition. The range percentile finds the room. Rule 6 and the new DD items (below) determine whether anything can fill the room inside the option window. And the screen stops wasting hours on mega-caps: an ABT-class name (2-3% earnings mover, $95 stock) structurally cannot pass Rule 5 and Rule 6 at the same time, so it exits the funnel at a glance now. The week-02 sector screens ran 19 healthcare and consumer names; 14 of them were Rule 5/6-impossible from the first data pull. That time is recoverable.

## FINDING 2: THE ZERO-SELL FILTER SELECTS FOR PENDING CAPITULATION

Rule 3 for calls: maximum 1 Sell rating. The logic was professional consensus behind the recovery.

Two problems, one statistical, one structural.

**Statistical:** Sell ratings are rare everywhere. Roughly half of all analyst ratings are Buys and Sells run in the single-digit percents. "Zero to one Sells" barely filters anything. It felt rigorous because it sounded strict; the data says almost every name we screened passed it.

**Structural, and this one cost us real money:** a stock at its 52-week low where every analyst still says Buy is a stock where the ratings have only one direction available. The analysts have not capitulated YET. Our entry then sits directly in the path of the downgrades. Check the ledger: CCL, NKE, and BSX, 3 of our 8 closed trades, all died from post-entry analyst capitulation. RBC cut NKE from $70 to $50 nineteen days after our entry. That $70 target was published before Nike's decline; it was not a floor, it was a countdown.

**Resolution, two new DD requirements, ratified tonight:**
- **Floor freshness:** every Rule 4 bear-floor target must be dated within 60 days AND published after the stock's decline. A pre-decline target is void for Rule 4 purposes. (Also entered as a standing decision in Tab 6.)
- **Ratings momentum:** no downgrades and no target cuts in the 30 days before entry, and consensus EPS estimate revisions flat-to-up over the same window. This extends the estimate-revision check we already wrote for puts to the calls side. A stock at lows with fresh cuts is a knife still falling; a stock at lows where estimates have stopped falling is a dislocation with a floor under it.

## FINDING 3: DD NEVER ASKED THE OPTION MARKET FOR ITS OPINION

Every DD doc we have written checks analyst targets, insider buys, earnings dates, news, and sector context. Not one of them checked what the options market itself was forecasting. The straddle price at our expiry IS the market's estimate of the total move. It was sitting in the same chain we pulled for Rule 5, every time, unread.

**New DD requirement, the implied-move cross-check:** at entry, compute the implied move from the near-earnings straddle. If our required move to breakeven exceeds the implied move, the market is pricing our play as a lottery, and the play caps at 3.5/5 conviction regardless of everything else. If required is inside implied, note the ratio. This is the forward-looking twin of Rule 6 (which is historical). Rule 6 asks "has this stock ever moved that much?" The cross-check asks "is anyone with money at stake pricing that move now?"

## FINDING 4: DD NEVER ASKED WHY THE STOCK IS DOWN

The screen treats all bottom-quartile stocks as the same object. The ledger says they are four different objects, and only one of them is tradeable:

1. **Event overreaction.** A dated shock (guidance cut, one bad print) knocked the stock down more than the fundamentals moved. BSX's April guidance cut, ABT's drift to lows against insider buying. Recoverable, and the recovery has a catalyst to attach to. **This is the only tradeable category.**
2. **Secular decline.** The business is losing share and the low keeps getting lower. NKE was this (share loss to newer brands), and we owned it anyway because every box checked. The boxes could not see the category.
3. **Macro beta.** The stock is down because its sector or the tape is down. No idiosyncratic correction is coming on earnings day; the catalyst cannot fix what the catalyst did not break.
4. **Commodity/cycle unwind.** The CALM screen-out already caught this once (egg-supply normalization is not a dislocation). That instinct is now a rule.

**New DD requirement:** every research doc names the decline category in one sentence, with evidence. Category 1 proceeds. Categories 2-4 fail the screen regardless of Rules 1-6. If the researcher cannot name the category, the answer is category 2 until proven otherwise.

## FINDING 5: SHORT INTEREST WAS A FOOTNOTE; IT IS ACTUALLY THE ENGINE

Current binder treatment: "high short interest is a tailwind for calls, note but do not penalize." A footnote.

But walk through what actually pays a far-OTM call inside 6 weeks: a gap. And what manufactures gaps on positive surprises: forced buying, meaning short covering. The only realized 5x in fund history (DKNG) rode a sentiment squeeze. LYFT, the one green position and the one Rule 6 pass in the open book, carries chronically high short interest. This is not decoration on the thesis. On cheap volatile names, it IS most of the thesis.

**Resolution:** short interest and days-to-cover move from footnote to mandatory DD line item for calls, with the float number and source cited. Not a pass/fail gate, but a conviction input: a category-1 dislocation with 15%+ short interest and a dated print is the exact shape of the trade this fund is built to catch. The screen should be actively hunting that shape rather than tripping over it.

## THE UPGRADED FUNNEL, CONSOLIDATED
*What a name must now survive between the screen and the binder tap. Ratified July 10.*

**Screen (minutes per name):**
1. Range percentile: bottom quartile for calls, top for puts, middle is out. (Rule 1, unchanged)
2. Confirmed earnings inside the window, 21+ DTE. (Rule 2, unchanged)
3. Ratings direction filter: calls max 1 Sell AND no downgrades/target cuts in 30 days; puts min 2 Sells or fresh Buy-to-Sell. (Rule 3, hardened)
4. Feasibility glance: can any strike plausibly pass Rule 5 AND Rule 6 together? If the stock is a $95 name that moves 2% on prints, stop here. (New)

**DD (the full doc, before any pitch):**
5. Decline/rally category named with evidence; only event overreaction (calls) or specific-disappointment setup (puts) proceeds. (New)
6. Rule 4 bear floor/bull ceiling: fresh targets only, dated within 60 days, post-move. (Hardened)
7. Rule 5 chain filter at the researched strike. (Unchanged)
8. Rule 6 reachability: required move vs median of last 4-8 earnings reactions, table included in the doc. (New, ratified in Part 1)
9. Implied-move cross-check: required move vs straddle-implied move; required > implied caps conviction at 3.5/5. (New)
10. Short interest, days-to-cover, float, cited. (Promoted)
11. Estimate revision direction, trailing 30 days, both directions. (Extended to calls)
12. Insider activity, Form 4 only, never 13F. (Unchanged, standing decision)
13. EV calculation and Kelly-bounded sizing per Tab 3. (Unchanged)

Bullxter's only comment on the thirteen-line funnel: "We used to need five yeses to buy a lottery ticket. Now we need thirteen yeses to buy a good one. Fine. But when a name survives all thirteen, nobody in this room is allowed to be timid about the size." Calxter noted, with equanimity, that Tab 3 already encodes exactly that. Bullxter said he knew, and that he was saying it anyway, for the record.

## GLOSSARY

- **Momentum (cross-sectional):** The century-old tendency of recent winners to keep winning and recent losers to keep losing over multi-week to multi-month horizons. The force Rule 1 trades against, now consciously.
- **Mean reversion:** The tendency of prices to snap back toward an average. Real but short-horizon and weak relative to momentum; insufficient to power a 6-week OTM option on its own.
- **52-week range percentile:** Where a stock sits between its 1-year low (0%) and high (100%). Rule 1's sorting key.
- **Analyst capitulation:** The pattern of analysts cutting ratings/targets only after a decline is well underway. A stock at lows with all-Buy ratings has capitulation ahead of it, not behind it.
- **Floor freshness:** New requirement that Rule 4 targets be dated within 60 days and published after the decline. A stale target is a countdown, not a floor.
- **Estimate revisions:** The direction consensus EPS forecasts have moved recently. Falling revisions into a print raise miss risk; stabilized revisions suggest the dislocation has found its floor.
- **Implied move:** The total move the options market prices for an event, readable from the near-expiry straddle. The forward-looking twin of Rule 6's historical check.
- **Straddle:** A call and put at the same strike and expiry; its combined price approximates the expected magnitude of the stock's move.
- **Short interest / days-to-cover:** Shares sold short as a percent of float, and how many days of average volume it would take shorts to buy back. The fuel gauge for squeezes, which are how far-OTM calls actually get paid.
- **Squeeze:** Forced short-covering that accelerates a rising price. DKNG's engine.
- **Secular decline:** A business losing ground structurally (category 2). Not a dislocation; not tradeable with calls no matter how many boxes check.
- **Gap vs grind:** A gap is a discontinuous repricing (what options need inside a window); a grind is a slow multi-quarter recovery (what beaten-down large caps actually do).
- **Form 4 / 13F:** Insider transaction filing vs institutional quarter-end holdings. Only Form 4 is an insider signal. Standing decision, June 17.
- **DTE:** Days to expiration.
- **Kelly criterion:** Growth-optimal bet sizing given edge and odds; the ceiling under which Tab 3's tiers sit.
