## GLOSSARY

**Annual report (vs quarterly):** Public companies report earnings four times a year (quarterly) and once as a full-year summary (annual). The June 30 call is Nike's annual report for FY2026. Annual reports include more forward-looking commentary, full-year guidance, and management perspective on the business trajectory than quarterly calls.

**Beat-then-fall:** When a company reports earnings that are better than analysts expected (a "beat") but the stock price still falls. This happens when the forward guidance -- what management says will happen next quarter -- disappoints, even if the current quarter was good. Nike experienced this in Q2 and Q3 FY2026. The market sells the future, not the past.

**Break-even:** The stock price at which the option becomes profitable at expiry. For a $50 call that cost $1.86, break-even is $51.86. Below that at expiry, the option loses money.

**Call option:** A contract giving the buyer the right (not obligation) to buy 100 shares of a stock at the strike price before the expiry date. The buyer pays a premium. If the stock never exceeds the strike, the premium is the total loss.

**China (Nike's exposure):** Greater China represents approximately 15% of Nike's total revenue. Nike lost meaningful market share in China to local brands (Li-Ning, Anta, Peak) during its DTC pivot when it cut wholesale partners. Recovery is expected to take 2-3 years. The stock has been pricing China as if it's the whole business -- it isn't.

**DTC (Direct-to-Consumer):** A retail strategy where a brand sells directly to customers through its own stores and website rather than through wholesale partners (like Dick's Sporting Goods or Foot Locker). Nike's previous management over-rotated to DTC between 2020-2024, cutting wholesale relationships that were critical to brand presence and revenue. Elliott Hill is rebuilding those relationships.

**Elliott Hill:** Nike's CEO as of late 2024. Previously worked at Nike for decades before retiring, then was brought back to lead the turnaround. His personal purchase of $2 million in Nike stock in April 2026 (at prices well above the current level) is a strong insider signal.

**EPS (Earnings Per Share):** Total company profit divided by shares outstanding. A standard measure of profitability. "Beating EPS" means the company earned more per share than Wall Street predicted.

**Ex-dividend date:** The date on which a stock stops trading with an upcoming dividend included in the price. Anyone who buys the stock on or after this date does not receive the next dividend payment. On June 1, NKE went ex-div for a $0.41 quarterly dividend, which caused the stock to open slightly lower than the prior close. This was expected and factored into entry timing (waited for mid-morning after ex-div selling cleared).

**FY2026 / FY2027:** Nike's fiscal year runs June 1 to May 31 (approximately). FY2026 ends May 2026 -- reported at the June 30 earnings call. FY2027 begins June 1, 2026. The June 30 call is the first chance for Elliott Hill to formally guide analysts on FY2027, which is the year his turnaround thesis either becomes visible or doesn't.

**Goldman Sachs downgrade:** In April 2026, Goldman Sachs changed their rating on NKE from Buy to Neutral and cut their price target to $52. This is the most pessimistic major institutional view on the stock. Their $52 target is the "bear case" for valuation purposes -- and it still sits above our $51.86 break-even.

**Iron Rule:** The Island Fund's self-imposed trading constraints. The relevant rule: no more than 50% of the fund in any single play. At $500, that's $250 maximum. $186 is within this limit.

**JP Morgan downgrade:** JP Morgan also moved to a cautious stance on NKE in the same period as Goldman, with a similar $52 target. Both banks are considered the most conservative major analysts covering the name.

**Q4 FY2026 guidance (pre-disclosed):** On March 31, 2026 (the Q3 earnings call), Nike management told the market to expect Q4 FY2026 revenue to fall 4% and China revenue to fall 20%. This was a negative surprise that caused the stock to fall 15.5% that day. By June 30, this guidance has already been fully absorbed by the market. The stock has already priced in the bad quarter.

**Sport Offense:** Elliott Hill's product strategy introduced on the Q4 FY2025 earnings call, which caused the stock to rise 10% during the call despite a difficult quarter. The strategy focuses on performance athletic products over lifestyle/fashion, and rebuilding wholesale distribution. The June 30 call will include the first full fiscal year update on how Sport Offense is performing.

**Strike price:** The fixed price at which the option gives the right to buy shares. The $50 strike means the contract allows purchasing NKE at $50 per share. If NKE is at $54 at expiry, the option is worth $4 (the difference).

**Wholesale channel:** Selling products through third-party retailers (Dick's Sporting Goods, Foot Locker, Nordstrom, etc.) rather than through the brand's own stores and website. Nike cut wholesale partners during its DTC push. The recovery in wholesale is one of the clearest signals that Hill's strategy is working -- Dick's Sporting Goods reporting +6% same-store comps on May 27 is a direct data point.

**World Cup (2026):** The FIFA Men's World Cup, hosted in the United States, Mexico, and Canada from June 11 to July 19, 2026. The largest single sporting event in the world by viewership. Nike supplies jerseys for six of the top ten teams. Nike has invested more in World Cup marketing than any event in company history. The tournament is entirely contained within Q1 FY2027 from Nike's perspective, but Hill will have 19 days of live World Cup data to reference when he gets on the June 30 call.
