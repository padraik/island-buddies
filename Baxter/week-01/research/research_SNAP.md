## GLOSSARY

**Ad platform rollback:** In 2022-2023, Snap redesigned its advertising platform in ways that made it harder for brands to measure ROI. Advertisers reduced spending. Snap eventually rolled back the changes and rebuilt the platform. Q2 FY2026 is the first quarter with a full three months of the rebuilt platform running without transition disruption.

**Bid-ask spread:** The gap between what a buyer will pay (bid) and what a seller will accept (ask) for an option. On cheap options like a $0.17 SNAP call, this spread can be $0.05-0.10 wide -- meaning a $0.17 ask might have a $0.10 bid. If you need to sell quickly, you might only get $0.10 even if the listed price was $0.17. Calxter flagged this as a real cost to factor in.

**Break-even:** The stock price at which the option becomes profitable at expiry. For a $7 call that costs $0.21, break-even is $7.21.

**Call option:** A contract giving the buyer the right (not obligation) to purchase 100 shares of a stock at the strike price before the expiry date. The premium paid is the maximum possible loss.

**CPM (Cost Per Mille):** The price an advertiser pays for 1,000 impressions (views) of their ad. Higher CPM = advertiser paying more per view = better revenue for the platform.

**DTC pivot (Snap context):** Unlike Nike, Snap's DTC issue was a product strategy mistake (the ad platform redesign) rather than a distribution strategy mistake. Both resulted in lost revenue and stock price declines. Both required a rollback to fix.

**EPS (Earnings Per Share):** Total company profit divided by shares outstanding. For Snap, EPS is negative (they are not yet consistently profitable). A Q1 EPS of -$0.05 vs an expected -$0.08 means they lost less money than expected -- which counts as a "beat."

**Expected value (EV):** A probability-weighted average of all possible outcomes. At a $0.17 entry, Calxter calculated positive EV on the SNAP $7C. At the actual $0.21 entry, the EV is slightly lower but still positive given the consensus-vs-break-even gap.

**Iron Rule:** The Island Fund's self-imposed trading rules. SNAP was not ruled out by the Iron Rule (three contracts at $0.21 = $63, well within limits). It was ruled out because the conviction rating (3/5) didn't meet the bar for Island Fund capital. Dad entered it with personal money outside the fund.

**Liquidity (options):** How easily an option can be bought or sold without significantly moving the price. Low-priced options on mid-cap stocks like SNAP can have wide bid-ask spreads and thin order books, making entry and exit less predictable than options on large-cap stocks.

**MAU (Monthly Active Users):** The number of unique users who opened an app at least once in a given month. Snap reports 932 million MAU. This is the base that determines advertising revenue potential.

**Neutral analyst rating:** A rating (sometimes called "Hold" at other firms) meaning analysts think the stock will roughly match the market return -- neither significantly outperform nor underperform. 9 of Snap's 12 analysts rate it Neutral. 3 have a Sell rating. Zero have a Buy. This is a weaker consensus than ABT (28 Strong Buy) or NKE (mix of Neutral and Buy).

**Q2 FY2026 (Snap):** April through June 2026. The last six weeks overlap with the FIFA World Cup (June 11 - July 19). This is the quarter that will be reported on July 16 -- the same day as ABT's earnings.

**ROI (Return on Investment):** How much an advertiser earns back relative to what they spent on ads. If an advertiser spends $10,000 on Snap ads and generates $15,000 in product sales, their ROI is 50%. When Snap's ad platform made ROI hard to measure, advertisers pulled spending.

**Strike price:** The fixed price at which the option gives the right to buy shares. The $7 strike means the contract allows buying SNAP at $7 per share. If SNAP is at $7.63 at expiry, the option is worth $0.63.

**TikTok competition:** TikTok targets the same 18-34 demographic as Snapchat. Both platforms compete for the same user attention and advertiser dollars. TikTok's continued growth represents a structural headwind for Snap's user engagement and advertiser demand. This is the main structural concern Bearxter raised -- it doesn't go away because of a good quarter.

**World Cup engagement (Snap):** Snap's core use case -- short video clips, filters, and direct messaging -- aligns with how younger users share live sporting moments. During major sporting events, Snap historically sees engagement spikes. The question is whether that engagement translates to higher advertiser CPMs and revenue in the Q2 financials.
