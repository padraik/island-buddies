# Both Sides
*June 27, 2026. Friday night. After 100 names.*

The number at the top of the page was zero.

Not zero entries -- zero advances. Five batches, 100 names across six days, and the puts column in the watch file showed nothing new that passed all five rules. Prime closed the batch 5 log and set the pen down.

He looked at the list.

COIN, $149, 3rd percentile. ZG, $31.18, 4th percentile. RBLX, $47.45, 7th percentile. UPST, $28.85, 8th percentile. LVS, $46.28, 11th percentile.

Five names that had died on Rule 1. *Top 20-25% of 52-week range.* These names weren't near their highs. They were near their lows.

He stared at LVS.

Las Vegas Sands. Down 34% from its 52-week high. Revenue up 25%. EPS up 73%. Macau EBITDA $633M and they'd said on the call they were targeting $700M. The company bought back $740M of its own stock at these prices. And it had failed the puts Rule 1 because the stock was at the bottom of its range, not the top.

*That is not a puts setup.*

*That is a calls setup.*

He sat with that for a moment. Then he opened a new page.

---

Bullxter was the first one to understand what he was doing.

"Oh," Bullxter said. "Oh, come on."

"Say it."

"We've been running a one-direction screen. For six days. 100 names." He pushed off the wall. "Every name that failed Rule 1 for puts failed because the stock was near its lows. And we just filed it and moved on. We didn't ask what a stock near its lows is actually good for."

"A calls play," Prime said.

"A calls play." He walked to the page. "LVS at the 11th percentile with Q2 earnings July 21. ZG at the 4th percentile with Q2 earnings August 5. RBLX at the 7th percentile. These are exactly the stocks we built a calls system to find. They were in the screen the whole time. We just weren't looking."

---

Bearxter was quiet for longer than usual.

When he spoke, he spoke to the list.

"Rule 3 inverts," he said. "We want near-zero Sell ratings for calls. LVS: 15 Buy, 5 Hold, zero Sell. ZG: 14 Buy, no Sell. RBLX: 1 Sell out of 40. They pass." He looked at Prime. "Rule 2 is the same. Earnings before expiry. LVS July 21 and ZG August 5 both land inside August 21. They pass."

"Rule 4," Bullxter said, already moving ahead.

"Rule 4 is the one I want to talk about." Bearxter sat. He did not usually sit. "The lowest Buy analyst target must be above call breakeven. LVS: lowest target in the range is $58. The $55C August 21 is trading at $0.63. Breakeven is $55.63. $58 is above $55.63. That one is clean."

"ZG," Macxter said from the corner. He had not moved. He had been looking at the screen since before the meeting started.

"ZG," Bearxter confirmed. "The aggregate data shows a lowest target of $40. Current price $31.18. The $40C August 21 is trading at $0.90. Breakeven is $40.90. If that $40 target belongs to a Buy analyst, $40 is not above $40.90. Rule 4 fails by $0.90."

"So we verify it," Bullxter said.

"Yes. We verify it. We do not advance ZG until we know whose $40 target that is and what rating it belongs to."

The room waited.

Prime pulled up the data.

It took less than a minute. Goldman Sachs, June 12. Rating: Neutral. Target lowered from $53 to $40. Neutral was Hold. A Hold analyst's target did not count for Rule 4. For Rule 4, they needed the most pessimistic Buy-rated analyst, and the most pessimistic Buy-rated analyst covering ZG was Wells Fargo, at $45, confirmed May 7.

$45 was above $40.90.

He put the pen down. "Goldman Sachs Neutral. Hold. Does not count."

Bearxter looked at the number for a moment.

"Then Rule 4 passes. $4.10 margin." He said it the same way he would have said it hadn't passed -- same tone, same pace. "Advance both."

---

Calxter had the page ready before anyone asked.

"LVS. $55C August 21 at $0.63. Breakeven $55.63. Required move from $47.14: 18%. I'll use 3 scenarios."

Nobody told him to continue. He continued.

"Scenario 1: Q2 beat, Macau EBITDA approaches $700M, stock re-rates to $60 on average. Probability 40%. Net gain per contract: $374. Scenario 2: inline or slight miss, stock stays $46 to $52, call expires worthless. Probability 55%. Loss per contract: $63. Scenario 3: China macro shock before earnings. Probability 5%. Loss per contract: $63."

He looked up. "EV: $149.60 minus $37.80. Positive $111.80."

"ZG. $40C August 21 at $0.90. Breakeven $40.90. Required move from $31.18: 31.2%. The required move is larger. Q1 showed revenue up 18% with the industry flat. Mortgage volume up 56%. If Q2 continues the share gain story and there is any macro signal on rates, the upside path is real."

He turned the page. "Scenario 1: Q2 beat with any rate catalyst, stock reaches $48 average. Probability 25%. Net gain per contract: $620. Scenario 2: inline, stock stays flat. Probability 60%. Loss: $90. Scenario 3: miss or rate shock, stock falls. Probability 15%. Loss: $90."

"EV: $155 minus $67.50. Positive $87.50."

He set the pen flat on the page. He was done.

"LVS is the stronger play on EV and on required move," he said. "ZG is viable. The required move is the honest cost of the larger discount from fair value."

---

Macxter spoke without turning from the window.

"China domestic stimulus was signaled in Q1. The execution lag is one quarter. If the program deploys into Q2, Macau mass market gaming absorbs it first -- that segment is Chinese middle-class tourists with more disposable income. It does not require VIP recovery. It does not require high-net-worth confidence. It requires the middle class to be spending." He paused. "The July 21 earnings call will have the first Q2 Macau data. If mass market held or grew against the VIP headwinds, the stock's entire discount narrative breaks."

"Rate environment for ZG," Bullxter said.

"ZG is the most rate-sensitive name we've touched. The June 25 MBA mortgage application data would already reflect the first week of June rate movement. If the purchase index moved up, the Q2 print reflects it. Warsh has not signaled. But the inflation data through May was softer than March. The market is starting to price a later cut. If that pricing becomes conviction before August 5, ZG reprices before the earnings catalyst even fires."

He came away from the window.

"Two separate macro stories. Different drivers. The correlated cap question is whether they're the same bucket. They're not identical -- Macau gaming and US housing don't move on the same signal. But both plays benefit from global risk appetite improving. Combined exposure: $63 plus $90 is $153. That's 24.9% of $614 reserve. Under the 35% cap."

"Unless DASH puts are entered at the same time," Bearxter said.

"Unless DASH puts are entered at the same time. Monitor."

---

Prime had been writing.

He stopped when Macxter finished. He looked at the two pages -- LVS on the left, ZG on the right. Five rules checked against each. EV positive on both. Chain data confirmed. Rule 4 verified to the source.

"This is what a unified screen produces," he said. "One pass. Both directions. Everything in the bottom quartile goes through the calls gate. Everything in the top quartile goes through the puts gate. The middle is out without discussion. We ran 100 names and found the puts plays we advanced. We should have been running the same 100 names and finding the calls plays at the same time. We weren't."

"We are now," Bullxter said.

"We are now." He wrote it at the top of a new tab in the binder. Not in his usual shorthand. Fully: *UNIFIED SCREEN -- TOP QUARTILE PUTS, BOTTOM QUARTILE CALLS, MIDDLE OUT.*

"Monday," Bullxter said. He was looking at the window now, not at Prime. "Michael is going to ask how we ran 100 names and came back with two plays. He's going to say that's two percent."

"Two percent is two plays we believe in," Prime said. "The other ninety-eight are not plays we settled for. They're plays we rejected because the rules said so."

"He knows that."

"He does. But he'll say it anyway."

Bullxter smiled, just slightly. "Yeah."

---

Bearxter spoke last, as he usually did when the meeting was closing.

"LVS leads the pitch. 18% required move, clean Rule 4 margin, earlier catalyst. If Michael has one yes in him, LVS earns it." He was looking at the chain data, not at the room. "$55.63 breakeven against a $58 floor. $2.37 of floor above us. I want that number said in the room on Monday. Not because it's big -- it isn't. Because Michael should hear that we ran the floor check, we found the source, and we know exactly where the Rule 4 tripwire sits."

"And if he wants ZG," Bullxter said.

"Then ZG. 31% is a real required move. He's going to ask about it. The answer is Goldman Sachs cut their target to $40 on June 12 with a Neutral rating, which does not count under our rules, and the lowest Buy-rated analyst in the universe is $45. Say it that specifically. Don't summarize it. The detail is the point."

Prime capped the pen.

"One more thing. If Michael asks why we're showing him calls plays after six days of puts screening, the answer is: we refined the screen. The calls system and the puts system were built separately. Running them in parallel in the same batch is new as of today. LVS and ZG are the first output of a better process."

"That's accurate," Calxter said.

"It's also a better story than 'we ran a hundred names and found two'," Bullxter said.

"Both things can be true," Prime said.

---

*He closed the binder.*

*LVS on Monday. ZG if there's appetite. The reserve sits at $614 and the fund goes to the meeting with something.*

*Two names. Clean rules. The island is still the point.*
