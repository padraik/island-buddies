## BEAR THESIS

**The specific Q2 2026 disappointment scenario:**

The Trade Desk built its 2024-2025 multiple partly on political ad spending — a massive one-time tailwind as campaigns poured money into programmatic digital channels. 2025 was the hangover year. 2026 was supposed to be the "organic return to growth" year.

Q2 2026 is the first quarter with clean comps against the post-2024-election environment. If organic CTV ad revenue growth (ex-political cycle) comes in below 20% YoY — the consensus is ~24-28% — the market has its first evidence that the CTV secular thesis is slower than priced.

Specific disappointment triggers:
- **CPM compression:** As more CTV inventory comes online (Peacock, Paramount+, Max all expanding ad tiers), the auction competition shifts from demand-constrained to supply-constrained. Lower CPMs = lower take rate for TTD.
- **Cookie deprecation delay:** If Google announces another delay in Chrome cookie phase-out, UID2's urgency fades. This doesn't hurt Q2 revenue directly but would hurt the Q2 call's narrative.
- **Net revenue retention:** If existing advertiser spend is growing below 105% (flat with prior year), churn or spend reduction is occurring. TTD needs consistent upsell.

**What the stock does on this scenario:** At 15-20x revenue with slowing growth, TTD reprices to 10-12x. At $82 and ~$5B revenue run rate, 10x = $50-60. Stock would reach $65-70 on a deceleration print, getting us close to but not quite through our $64.23 breakeven. A worse print (growth below 18%) would push through.

This is why the EV is only marginally positive: the bear scenario needs to be specifically bad (not just modestly disappointing) to profit at the $65 strike.

## GLOSSARY

**Demand-side platform (DSP):** Software that allows advertisers and agencies to buy digital advertising in automated real-time auctions across multiple ad exchanges. The Trade Desk is the dominant independent DSP in programmatic advertising. "Independent" means it doesn't also run an ad exchange (unlike Google, which owns both DSP and exchange, creating conflicts of interest).

**CPM (cost per thousand impressions):** The standard unit pricing for display and video advertising. Higher CPMs indicate stronger demand relative to supply for that audience or placement. CTV CPMs are typically 3-5× higher than social media CPMs because of the perceived quality of the viewing environment.

**Take rate:** The percentage of total advertiser spend that the DSP keeps as revenue. For The Trade Desk, take rate has been approximately 19-21% of media spend. If advertisers negotiate harder or move spending to Google/Amazon, the take rate compresses.

**Net revenue retention (NRR):** The year-over-year revenue growth from existing customers only (excluding new customer acquisition). NRR >100% means existing customers spent more this year than last. NRR <100% means existing customers reduced spend — a churn signal. TTD typically targets NRR >100% from its enterprise advertiser base.

**Unified ID 2.0 (UID2):** The Trade Desk's proposed industry standard for tracking users across websites using hashed, consented email addresses. Designed to replace third-party browser cookies. Adoption depends on publishers and advertisers accepting UID2 as the standard — and on Google actually following through on cookie deprecation.
