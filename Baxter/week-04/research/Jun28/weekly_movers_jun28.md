## MACRO CONTEXT FOR THE WEEK

**What happened to the market overall:**
The week was driven by two competing forces. First, a broad AI demand skepticism narrative took hold Monday-Tuesday, amplified by the South Korean KOSPI chip index falling nearly 10% in a single session and reports of a potential OpenAI IPO delay. Nasdaq dropped 2.2% Tuesday and the S&P 500 shed 1.4%. Technology stocks took the bulk of the damage.

Second, an Iran-US deal roadmap announcement (US and Tehran agreed on a path toward a nuclear deal within 60 days) sent oil prices down hard: Brent crude fell 4.33% to $73.74, WTI fell 3.92% to $70.34. The oil drop cascaded into lower Treasury yields -- the 10-year note fell below 4.5% -- triggering a sector rotation toward REITs, healthcare, and small caps. By Thursday, 63% of S&P 500 stocks were trading above their 50-day moving average, up from 50% at the start of June.

The macro backdrop for the week: tech fear driving broad market weakness mid-week, partially offset by rate-sensitive sector strength from the Iran oil shock.

## BIGGEST MOVERS

### 1. WEN (Wendy's) -- +25-42% Wednesday, June 25

**What happened:**
Wendy's stock surged 25% on massive volume Wednesday, with intraday peaks reaching +42% before trading halts. Net retail buying on the day was more than 50 times Wendy's 20-day average. The move continued Thursday morning in premarket, extending further.

**Why:**
Classic short squeeze catalyzed by two events arriving simultaneously:

First, a legitimate management catalyst: on Tuesday June 24, Wendy's announced Steve Cirulis as new CFO, effective immediately. Bob Wright (new CEO from May 2026) and Cirulis previously ran Potbelly together, where they orchestrated a 500%+ stock turnaround from near bankruptcy. The professional track record was real and specific.

Second, Reddit's WallStreetBets launched a "Save Wendy's" campaign that converted the leadership news into a meme narrative. With Wendy's short interest at approximately 34% of float -- one of the highest short interest ratios among consumer stocks -- the retail buying blitz turned into a short squeeze. Shorts had to buy to cover, adding fuel.

**Could we have predicted this?**
Not the specific Reddit catalyst. Nobody predicts when WSB decides to mobilize. But the SETUP was recognizable:
- Short interest 34% of float (>10% is already a risk flag in our framework; 34% is extreme)
- Stock near multi-year lows (beaten-down fundamental picture)
- Credible new management team with a documented turnaround track record
- Fast food sector under pressure = lots of media coverage = retail awareness

The management announcement on Tuesday June 24 was the genuine catalyst. A sophisticated screener looking for "beaten-down stock + extreme short interest + credible new management" would have flagged WEN for a short-notice call on June 24 afternoon.

**What it means for our system:**
Our Iron Rules don't include short squeeze plays. We don't have a short interest tracking step. WEN-style moves require: (1) a fundamental catalyst that gives the retail narrative a foundation, (2) extreme short interest creating the squeeze mechanism. We could add a short interest flag to the screening process: any stock with short interest >20% of float is tagged as "short squeeze risk" -- not necessarily a play, but worth knowing about. If a management catalyst arrives on a 20%+ short stock at our lows, that's a one-day callout rather than a multi-week research play. This is a different playbook from our Iron Rules setup.

### 2. MU (Micron) -- Down 11.4% Tuesday, Up 15%+ after hours Wednesday

**What happened:**
Micron had the most instructive two-day move of the week. On Tuesday June 23, amid the broader AI selloff, MU dropped 11.4% to $1,074.60. The decline was driven not by Micron-specific news but by macro chip fear: KOSPI's Korean chip index cratered 10%, TSMC fell 5.2%, and NVDA fell 3.2%. Micron fell hardest because it was reporting Q3 2026 earnings after Wednesday's close, making it the focal point for AI demand uncertainty.

Then Wednesday June 24 after hours, Micron reported:
- EPS: $25.11 vs $20.20 expected (+24.3% beat)
- Revenue: $41.46B vs $35.84B expected (+15.7% beat)
- Gross margin: 84.9% (vs 74.9% prior quarter, 39% a year ago)
- Q4 revenue guidance: ~$50B vs $43.58B consensus (+14.7% guidance beat)
- Data center sales up 7x YoY to $11.5B
- 16 long-term supply agreements, $22B in committed financial obligations

Stock jumped 15%+ in after-hours trading. Micron's full-year stock gain exceeded 700%.

**Could we have predicted this?**
The 11.4% Tuesday dip was specifically predictable as a buying opportunity for anyone who (a) already believed in the AI memory demand thesis and (b) recognized that the selloff was macro/sentiment-driven rather than Micron-specific. The KOSPI selloff hit Korean chip stocks that have different exposure than Micron -- Micron is the US DRAM leader with 16 locked long-term supply contracts that were signed before earnings. The fear of AI demand slowing did not apply to a company with $22B already contractually committed.

What would have been needed to capitalize: recognizing that the Tuesday selloff was macro contamination of a stock with locked-in revenue, buying calls (or stock) on Tuesday afternoon, and holding through Wednesday's close.

In terms of our Iron Rules: MU at $1,074 on Tuesday was not at the bottom 25% of its range (it was near highs after the 700% run). So our calls framework wouldn't have flagged it. The play would have been: (a) earnings beat thesis (aggressive buy-the-dip-into-earnings, not our style), or (b) shorter-duration calls, which is exactly what our August expiry structure doesn't support well for names near highs.

**What it means for our system:**
Two lessons.

First, pre-earnings macro contamination creates entry windows. When a stock drops 10%+ the day before earnings due to sector contagion (not company news), and you have strong fundamental conviction, that's a buy window. Our system doesn't have a "pre-earnings dip" tracker. We should be watching our high-conviction names for this exact setup: "did this stock just fall 8%+ on macro, with earnings tomorrow?"

Second, Micron's margin expansion from 39% to 84.9% in one year is an extraordinary fundamental. Gross margin nearly doubling in four quarters is an indicator of pricing power -- when DRAM is in shortage (demand exceeds supply), Micron charges premium prices. The supply/demand cycle for DRAM chips is a leading indicator of Micron's earnings. We didn't track MU because it was near highs and not in our calls zone. But the DRAM cycle intelligence belongs in the system.

### 3. SPCX (SpaceX) -- Down 23% from IPO peak over the week

**What happened:**
SpaceX (SPCX) had IPO'd on June 12 and rallied to make Elon Musk the world's first trillionaire within a week. By the week of June 23, a three-day selloff erased over $600 billion in market value, with the stock falling 16.4% on Monday June 22 alone (the largest single-day drop since debut).

**Why:**
Primary trigger: SpaceX announced it was raising at least $20 billion via its first-ever investment-grade bond issuance to fund AI infrastructure ambitions. The market interpreted this as: (a) SpaceX needs enormous capital to compete in AI, (b) the existing equity is being leveraged to fund spending that doesn't yet produce returns, (c) the AI capex story is getting more expensive, not less. Combined with the broader Nasdaq AI selloff mid-week, the bond announcement broke the post-IPO euphoria narrative.

Secondary factor: post-IPO volatility is structural. A stock that runs 570%+ in 10 days has no natural support level -- every price between IPO and the all-time high is held entirely by momentum buyers with no cost basis. When momentum reverses, there's no floor until the stock finds fundamental buyers.

**Could we have predicted this?**
Yes. The bond issuance announcement was the specific trigger, but the structural vulnerability was visible without that catalyst: (1) -23% from an all-time high reached 10 days after IPO is normal post-IPO behavior for high-sentiment stocks; (2) Any capital raise (bonds, secondaries) after a short-cycle IPO rally signals "we need more money than the IPO raised" -- this is fundamentally bearish sentiment; (3) A stock at 570% above its IPO price has no support structure from fundamental buyers.

What our system would need: a "post-IPO puts" strategy. When a newly-listed stock (< 90 days from IPO) has rallied 100%+ from IPO price, there is often a pullback phase as post-IPO lockup selling, secondary offerings, and bond issuances erode the supply constraint that drove the initial rally. SPCX at $180/share after a $31 IPO was exactly this setup. The bond issuance was the accelerant, but the pullback was predictable.

**What it means for our system:**
We don't currently screen IPO-adjacent stocks. Our Iron Rules require a 52-week range -- new IPOs don't have one. A "fresh IPO puts" play would need different rules: time since IPO (<90 days), % rally since IPO (>100%), analyst consensus lower than current price (which is often true for fresh IPOs without coverage). This is a different framework, not an Iron Rules extension. Flag for future development.

### 4. NVDA / TSMC -- Down 3-5% on Tuesday, partial recovery

**What happened:**
Nvidia fell 3.2% to $201.97 and Taiwan Semiconductor fell 5.2% to $443.35 on Tuesday, as the AI demand skepticism narrative hit all chip stocks. The KOSPI South Korean chip index fell nearly 10% in a single day, and that global selloff cascaded into US semis.

**Why:**
The stated concern: AI infrastructure spend (estimated at $500B+ globally) is creating demand for chips that has exceeded supply -- but the question is whether the *applications* built on that infrastructure will generate revenue fast enough to justify the cap-ex. If OpenAI delays its IPO (reported that week), it signals that even the largest AI consumer isn't ready for public markets yet. That fear compressed the AI multiple across the sector.

**Could we have predicted this?**
The specific Korea trigger was unpredictable. But the broader dynamic -- AI infrastructure stocks trading at elevated multiples carry heightened sensitivity to any AI demand data point -- was well understood. When any piece of news (OpenAI IPO delay, a minor miss from a Korean chipmaker, a note from a bearish analyst) hits this narrative, the stocks compress 3-10% in a day. That's a known risk premium in semi stocks.

**What it means for our system:**
When we screen semiconductor stocks for puts plays, we need to account for the reverse: these stocks also bounce 15% in a single day on good news (Micron's earnings were announced the same week). The volatility cuts both ways. For the puts side: semi stocks near highs (NVDA at $215, TSMC at $468) could be puts candidates -- but the framework requires sustained Sell ratings, and no semi analyst has sustained a Sell on NVDA at this point. For the calls side: if NVDA pulls back 20-30% on narrative fear (like Tuesday), it could enter our calls zone.

## GLOSSARY

**Short interest / float:** Short interest measures how many shares have been borrowed and sold short by traders betting the stock falls. "Short interest as a % of float" compares those shorted shares to the total shares available for trading. At 34% of float (as in WEN), roughly one-third of available shares are held by short sellers who must eventually buy back to close their positions. When a catalyst causes buyers to rush in, those short sellers also become forced buyers (to limit losses), amplifying the upward move. This "short squeeze" mechanism can create moves 5-10x larger than the fundamental catalyst alone would justify.

**DRAM supply cycle:** DRAM (Dynamic Random Access Memory) is the memory chip in servers, PCs, and AI accelerators. The industry is oligopolistic (Micron, Samsung, SK Hynix control 95%+ of supply). Every 3-7 years, the industry goes through a cycle: oversupply (prices crash, margins go negative) followed by undersupply (prices spike, margins expand dramatically). The AI infrastructure buildout beginning in 2024-2026 pulled forward data center DRAM demand far ahead of supply growth, creating the current supercycle. Micron's margin expansion from 39% to 84.9% in four quarters is the financial expression of a supply-demand imbalance in their favor. This margin level is not sustainable indefinitely -- it will revert when Samsung and SK Hynix add capacity. The investment question is how long the current supercycle lasts.

**Post-IPO lockup:** When a company IPOs, early investors (founders, VC funds, employees) typically cannot sell shares for 90-180 days post-IPO. This lockup prevents the supply of shares from immediately crushing the price after listing. When the lockup expires, early investors often sell some portion of their holdings, creating a predictable supply increase. For SpaceX, the lockup had not expired yet -- but bond issuances and secondary offerings can create similar supply signals before the lockup date.

**Macro contamination (pre-earnings):** When a stock drops due to sector-wide fear just before its own earnings announcement, the drop is "contaminated" by macro noise rather than company-specific information. MU's 11.4% drop on Tuesday was macro contamination: the Korean chip selloff (different companies, different supply chains) drove fear that spread to Micron. But Micron's own revenue was locked via 16 long-term contracts -- the Korean selloff had no direct bearing on Micron's Q3 results. Recognizing macro contamination vs. company-specific deterioration is one of the most consistently profitable pattern distinctions in short-term trading.
