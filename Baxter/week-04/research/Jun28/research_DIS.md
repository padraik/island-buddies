## THE COMPANY

Walt Disney Company operates three reporting segments:

- **Entertainment (45% of revenue):** Streaming (Disney+, Hulu, ESPN+) plus linear networks (ABC, Disney Channel, FX, National Geographic) plus film studios. Disney+ achieved streaming profitability in Q4 FY2024 and has maintained it, driven by price increases and ad-tier subscriber growth. Hulu remains the anchor of older-demo streaming. Studios: Marvel, Lucasfilm, Pixar, Disney Animation.

- **Experiences (50% of revenue):** Domestic theme parks (Walt Disney World, Disneyland), international parks (Paris, Tokyo, Hong Kong, Shanghai), Disney Cruise Line, merchandise. Parks are the most profitable segment on a margin basis. Consumer pressure from higher rates and inflation has slightly slowed per-capita spending growth.

- **Sports (5% of revenue):** ESPN linear + ESPN+. In Fall 2025, Disney launched the ESPN flagship (a direct-to-consumer premium ESPN streaming product offering access to every live sports event ESPN holds rights to). Q3 FY2026 earnings on August 12 will include the first two full quarters of ESPN flagship data.

The fiscal year ends September 30. Q3 FY2026 = April through June 2026.

**Why DIS is at 21st pct of its 52-week range ($92.19-$124.69):**
The stock peaked at $124.69 when streaming profitability was first confirmed and the ESPN DTC launch was still new catalyst. Since then, it has pulled back on: (1) slower-than-expected ESPN flagship subscriber growth in Q1 FY2026, (2) parks per-capita spending softening under consumer pressure, (3) broader consumer discretionary multiple compression from Warsh rates. At $99, the market is pricing "recovery stalling" rather than "recovery progressing."

**Q3 FY2026 catalyst (Aug 12, 2026):** First quarter with full Disney+/Hulu price increase cycle anniversaried AND two full quarters of ESPN flagship data available. If subscriber retention held post-price increase (ARPU beats) and ESPN flagship subscriber growth is on track, the streaming profitability story re-accelerates. That reprices DIS from "recovery stalling" to "recovery on track."

## MACXTER'S MACRO VIEW

**Consumer discretionary and rate environment:**

Warsh-era rates (elevated through H1 2026) have compressed theme park and entertainment spending at the margin. Disney's parks are partially protected by long booking lead times -- families who booked Walt Disney World 6 months ago don't cancel because rates are high. But per-capita spending once in the park (food, merch, upcharges) is more elastic and has softened.

The streaming business is largely rate-insensitive. A $14/month Disney+ subscription is not a discretionary purchase for a family with young children. This is the structural protection in the thesis: even in a rate-pressured environment, streaming ARPU can grow through price discipline.

**ESPN DTC and the sports rights cycle:**

Disney holds rights to NFL playoff games, NBA, WNBA, College Football Playoff, and all ESPN cable properties. The ESPN flagship's value proposition improves as the rights portfolio is used to market the DTC product. The next major rights renewal question is NBA (new deal kicked in 2025) -- Disney/ESPN retained the majority of games. This locks in a multi-year anchor for ESPN flagship growth. Macxter views this as a multi-year tailwind that Q3 data is just beginning to surface.

**China exposure (minimal):**

Hong Kong Disneyland and Shanghai Disney are small contributors to Experiences revenue. US-China trade tension does not materially affect DIS, unlike LVS or BABA. Disney's macro exposure is primarily US consumer, secondarily international tourism to US parks. No significant geopolitical risk vector.

**Macro summary:** Rate environment is a mild parks headwind that's already in the stock. Streaming is largely macro-immune. ESPN DTC is a multi-year tailwind beginning to show. Net macro: neutral to mildly supportive.

## GLOSSARY

**Disney+ ARPU (Average Revenue Per User):** The average monthly revenue Disney earns per Disney+ subscriber, across all plans (ad-supported and ad-free). Rising ARPU = subscribers are accepting price increases AND/OR subscribers are migrating to higher-priced ad-free tiers. ARPU is the key streaming profitability metric -- more important than raw subscriber count once the subscriber base is large. Disney has grown ARPU through two consecutive price cycles.

**ESPN flagship (DTC):** Disney's direct-to-consumer sports streaming product, offering access to the full ESPN rights library (NFL, NBA, College Football, baseball, etc.) without a cable subscription. Distinct from ESPN+ (a lower-cost product with secondary content). The flagship launched Fall 2025 at approximately $39.99/month (estimated) or bundled with Disney+/Hulu. Its subscriber trajectory in the first two full quarters (Q2 and Q3 FY2026) will determine whether DTC sports can replace the declining affiliate fee revenue from cord-cutting.

**Cord-cutting:** The ongoing trend of consumers canceling traditional cable/satellite TV subscriptions and replacing linear content with streaming services. Linear TV is Disney's legacy distribution model -- affiliate fees from cable companies (paying per-subscriber fees for ESPN, ABC, etc.) represent billions in annual revenue. As cord-cutting accelerates, affiliate fee revenue declines. Disney's strategy is to replace affiliate fee revenue with direct-to-consumer streaming revenue. Q3 FY2026 will show how close the replacement rate is to the decline rate.

**Rule 4 (calls):** The lowest Buy-rated analyst price target must be above the call breakeven. For DIS: lowest BUY target $123 is $7.15 above the $115.85 breakeven. This means the most pessimistic professional who still rates DIS a Buy thinks it's worth at least $7.15 more than our breakeven. Monitoring note: if any Buy-rated analyst drops their target to $115.85 or below during the hold period, Rule 4 is breached and the position must be exited same day.

**Per-capita spending (parks context):** Total in-park revenue divided by the number of park visitors. Includes ticket revenue, food, merchandise, and upsell products (Lightning Lane, dessert parties, etc.). Disney has consistently grown per-capita spending for a decade through premium upsell offerings. High interest rates reduce the average consumer's willingness to spend on upsells once inside the park, even though they typically won't cancel a family vacation already paid for. A per-capita spending miss is the most likely single-item risk in the Q3 parks segment.
