## THE BEAR THESIS

Airlines are a deceptively simple business: revenue minus cost equals margin. Two variables drive almost everything on the top line — how full the planes are (load factor) and how much each seat earns per mile (RASM). United's bear thesis centers on Q3 guidance:

**The specific Q2 2026 disappointment scenario:**

1. **RASM guidance for Q3 below consensus:** Q3 2026 (July-September) is peak summer travel. If management guides RASM flat-to-down vs. Q3 2025 (consensus expecting +2-4%), the market re-prices UAL from "growth airline" to "yield has plateaued."

2. **Fuel cost roll-off:** UAL's fuel hedges roll off at specific intervals. If crude prices spiked in Q1-Q2 2026 (Iran tensions), the hedges being reset now are at higher prices. Management would guide Q3-Q4 fuel cost per gallon higher, compressing estimated EBITDA margins by 3-5 percentage points.

3. **Corporate travel softening:** Interest rates at 3.5-3.75% (Warsh) are compressing CFO budgets. Corporate travel policies are getting stricter. UAL has significant first-class and business-class domestic capacity priced for corporate demand. Softening here hits the highest-margin inventory first.

4. **International yield normalization:** UAL runs significant transatlantic routes (Europe, Middle East). If USD strength reduces European traveler purchasing power in dollar terms, or if the Iran situation makes Middle East routes more operationally uncertain, international yield guidance gets conservative.

**What the stock does:** On a guide-down print like this, airlines reprice 15-25% in one session. The earnings call itself moves the stock — management commentary on Q3 is as important as the Q2 reported numbers. UAL at $87 falling 22% = $67.86. That is below our $69 breakeven.

## GLOSSARY

**Revenue per available seat mile (RASM):** Total revenue divided by available seat miles (ASMs = seats × miles flown). If RASM is growing, airlines are filling planes at higher prices. If flat or declining, either load factor is falling (empty seats) or pricing power is eroding. The single most important airline revenue metric.

**Cost per available seat mile (CASM):** Total operating costs divided by ASMs. Subtract fuel from CASM to get "ex-fuel CASM" — the part management can control. Fuel is the largest variable. The spread between RASM and CASM = operating margin per seat mile.

**Fuel hedge:** Airlines pre-purchase fuel at fixed prices using swaps and options, typically 12-18 months forward. A hedge "rolling off" means the old fixed-price contract expires and is replaced with current market prices. If crude rose while the old hedge was in place, the new hedge is at a higher price — a margin headwind that hits future quarters, not the current one.

**Load factor:** Percentage of available seats actually filled. 100% load factor = every seat occupied. Airlines target 85-90% load factors at prices above breakeven. If load factor is high but RASM is low, they're filling seats with discounted fares — unsustainable.

**Polaris (United's business class):** UAL's premium intercontinental business class product. Polaris seats generate 8-12× the revenue of economy seats per flight. Corporate contract rates for Polaris determine United's international yield trajectory. Any softening in corporate travel reduces Polaris occupancy disproportionately.

**MileagePlus (United's loyalty program):** UAL sells miles to Chase, which distributes them via co-branded credit cards. This "miles sale" to Chase is now a multi-billion dollar annual cash stream that doesn't depend on passengers flying. Provides earnings floor independent of yield cycles. Bears note that the program's value is only as good as consumer credit card spending, which is rate-sensitive.
