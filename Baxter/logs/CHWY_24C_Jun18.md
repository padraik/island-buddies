## THE SETUP

Chewy sells pet food and pet supplies online. That sentence undersells it. Chewy is what Amazon would look like if Amazon only sold things for dogs and cats and had better customer service. Their Autoship program -- automatic repeat delivery on a subscription schedule -- was the entire thesis. When a dog owner signs up for Autoship, Chewy has predictable recurring revenue on that customer for as long as the dog is alive. The customer acquisition cost is a one-time event. The revenue stream is multi-year.

The metrics reflected this. Autoship and Other revenue: $2.5 billion in the trailing twelve months, representing 78.4% of total net sales. Free cash flow positive. The business model was structurally sound.

The stock told a different story. CHWY was at $21.02 on June 1. The 52-week range ran from $16.22 to $38.10. At $21.02, the stock was at 22% of its range. The gap from its 52-week high was more than 44%.

The reason: EPS was down 42.9% year over year. That number stops most research in its tracks. A 43% earnings decline on a consumer discretionary subscription business is a flag, not a footnote.

Digging in: the EPS decline was driven by increased investment spending. Chewy was building out distribution centers, expanding its veterinary clinic footprint (Chewy Vet Care), and absorbing integration costs from its pharmacy business. None of this was hidden. Management had signaled it. The margin compression was deliberate and bounded.

Analysts: 28 covering. 13 Buy, 11 Hold, 4 Sell. Not a clean screen. Four Sell ratings in a 28-analyst universe is unusual -- typically a consensus has one or zero. The Sell ratings were real and reflected genuine concern about EPS trajectory.

Bear floor on the instrument: $28 -- the lowest Buy-side target. Breakeven: $24.56. Gap: +$3.44. Rule 4 passed.

But Rule 3 did not. The system requires near-zero Sell ratings. Four was not near-zero.

Conviction: 3/5. The thesis was interesting. The instrument was passable. The analyst structure had a flaw. One contract. $56 at risk.

## GLOSSARY

**Autoship:** Chewy's subscription delivery program. Customers set up a recurring delivery schedule for pet food and supplies at a slight discount. Once enrolled, Autoship customers have significantly higher lifetime value and lower churn than one-time buyers. Autoship and Other revenue represented 78.4% of Chewy's net sales.

**EPS down 42.9%:** Chewy's earnings per share declined 42.9% year over year due to intentional investment in distribution infrastructure, veterinary clinic expansion (Chewy Vet Care), and pharmacy integration costs. The company treated this as bounded investment spending with a defined end, not structural margin deterioration.

**Four Sell ratings:** Rule 3 requires near-zero Sell ratings among the covering analyst universe. Four Sell ratings in 28 is unusual and meaningful -- it means four credible professional analysts, with access to management and channel data, concluded the stock is likely to fall from current levels. This is a genuine signal. It does not make the position wrong, but it does create a structural tension the system is designed to flag.

**Capital reallocation:** The act of selling a lower-conviction position to free capital for a higher-conviction opportunity. In the Island Fund context, limited capital means positions compete for deployment. The conviction scoring system exists to make this decision mechanical: the highest-conviction setup that passes all Iron Rules gets the capital first.

**3/5 conviction:** An internal rating indicating a valid thesis with a meaningful structural imperfection. For CHWY: valid Autoship thesis, valid bear floor above breakeven, valid analyst consensus -- but four Sell ratings and EPS decline created a specific vulnerability. Not disqualifying. But not a 4/5.

**Pivot outcome:** CHWY cost $23 to exit. DKNG returned $251. The difference is $274 in outcome that was made possible by following the conviction hierarchy. The lesson is not that every pivot works out -- it is that the system identified DKNG as structurally superior before the outcome was known, and acting on that assessment was correct.
