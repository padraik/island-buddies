## THE SETUP

The bear thesis on Lyft was autonomous vehicles. Waymo would destroy it. Drivers would become unnecessary. Consumers would use the Waymo app directly. Lyft would become irrelevant.

The bears were right about autonomous vehicles. They were wrong about what that means for Lyft.

In late 2025, Lyft signed a fleet management partnership with Waymo for Nashville. Waymo owns the vehicles and operates the autonomous driving software. Lyft manages the fleet on the ground, handles customer service, and provides the booking infrastructure. The rides are bookable through both apps. Lyft earns revenue from every autonomous booking.

Lyft doesn't need to own the cars. They need to own the demand. Twenty-eight-point-three million active riders in Q1 2026 -- a Q1 record. Gross bookings up 19% year over year. Adjusted EBITDA up 25%. Net income positive at $14.2 million. These are not the numbers of a company being disrupted out of existence.

The stock was at $14.32 on June 18 -- at 12.7% of its 52-week range ($12.46 to $25.54). The 52-week range reflected a market that priced Lyft as a disruption casualty. The disruption had been answered with a partnership. The stock had not processed the answer.

Forty-three analysts covered Lyft. Forty-three had targets above the current price. One analyst -- Bank of America, Underperform at $14 -- believed the stock was worth exactly what it was trading for. The bear floor among Buy analysts: BMO at $20, $3.10 above the $16.90 breakeven.

All five Iron Rules passed.

## GLOSSARY

**Waymo partnership:** Alphabet's autonomous vehicle subsidiary supplies self-driving cars; Lyft manages the fleet and provides booking infrastructure in Nashville. Rides are bookable through both apps. Lyft earns a commission on every autonomous booking without owning or operating a vehicle. This is the structural change the bears did not model.

**Distribution layer:** In platform economics, the entity that connects supply with demand. Lyft's value in the autonomous vehicle future is not the car -- it is 28.3 million active riders who have the app installed and trust the service. Waymo has supply. Lyft has demand. That combination produces a partnership, not a displacement.

**Bear floor:** Lowest price target among Buy/Outperform analysts only. BofA's $14 Underperform target is excluded. The lowest Buy target is BMO at $20. Breakeven $16.90 is $3.10 below the bear floor. Rule 4 passes with meaningful margin.

**BofA Underperform at $14:** Bank of America's analyst rating. The only institutional bearish voice on the stock, at exactly the current price. Not excluded from the conviction assessment -- it is the reason this entry is 4/5 rather than 4.5/5. If BofA upgrades, the last institutional bear has capitulated.

**Active riders:** 28.3 million in Q1 2026, a Q1 record. The metric that defines Lyft's demand-side value. Even as the stock traded at $14, the underlying rider base was growing. The distribution advantage compounds with each quarter.

**Iran deal tailwind (June 18):** Oil price decline from Iranian crude re-entering the market. Rideshare unit economics improve: cheaper fuel means lower effective cost of driving, better driver retention, and consumer preference for rideshare over personal vehicle use increases when gas is cheap. Secondary to the earnings catalyst, but real.

**Nashville AV launch:** The Waymo-Lyft partnership's proof-of-concept market. Nashville was chosen deliberately -- large entertainment district, significant airport volume, and a demographically attractive AV test market. Q2 2026 is the first full operational quarter.

**Adjusted EBITDA:** Lyft's cash-generating capacity after removing non-cash and one-time items. $132.8 million in Q1 2026, up 25% year over year. Growing EBITDA on a stock at 52-week lows means the market is discounting forward risk (autonomous vehicle disruption), not current performance.
