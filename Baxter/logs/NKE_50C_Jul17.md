## THE SETUP

Nike is the most recognizable sports brand on earth. It is also a company that has been in a self-inflicted strategic crisis since 2020, when the previous management team decided to build a direct-to-consumer platform and systematically cut the wholesale relationships that had built the brand for decades.

The results were predictable in retrospect. Dick's Sporting Goods. Foot Locker. DSW. Nordstrom. Nike walked away from all of them in pursuit of their own app and their own stores. The wholesale partners found other brands. The brand's presence in physical retail collapsed. Revenue slowed, then declined, then declined further.

By June 1, 2026, the stock was at $45.93. In 2021 it had traded at $150. In 2022 it was at $80. The stock had lost 69% of its value from the high. More importantly: it was at a 12-year low, a price it had not seen since 2014.

The new CEO, Elliott Hill, came in at the end of 2024 and said the previous team had lost the plot. He called the strategy "Sport Offense" -- recommitting to performance athletics, rebuilding wholesale partnerships, letting the products drive the brand rather than the other way around. Two consecutive quarters of running shoe revenue up 20%. North America wholesale up 9%. Dick's Sporting Goods reporting 6% same-store growth -- the Nike rack filling back up.

And then there was the World Cup.

Nike makes the jerseys for six of the top ten national teams: France, England, Brazil, the Netherlands, the United States, and others. The 2026 World Cup was hosted in North America -- the first in the Western Hemisphere in 32 years. Nike had committed more marketing spend to this tournament than any event in company history.

On June 30, 2026, Nike would report Q4 FY2026 -- the full year results. Elliott Hill would have 19 days of live World Cup sales data on the table in front of him.

Nine analysts rated the stock at Goldman Sachs and JP Morgan -- both conservative shops -- had NKE at $52. That was the most pessimistic credible view on the stock. Our breakeven was $51.86. The bears were at $52. The bears were above the breakeven.

The $50C Jul17 cost $1.86. One contract: $186 at risk.

That was 37% of the fund at the time. That was over the $1.00 per share cap that would later become Rule 5. The position was entered before that rule existed in its current form.

## THE STORY

The thesis was built over a weekend. Saturday night, the binder was open to Tab 2. The original model had been built around a $49 strike -- and on Sunday morning, Baxter checked the chain and the $49 strike did not exist. Nike trades in $2.50 increments: $45, $47.50, $50. There was no $49 option. The entire pitch had been built on an instrument that was not real.

Bearxter wrote it at the top of the legal pad and underlined it twice: check the chain before the pitch, not after.

The $50C at $1.84-1.89 was the closest available. At $500 in the fund, the sizing was 37.2% of capital. Within Iron Rule limits -- no single position above 50%.

But the math was tighter than it looked. Break-even at $51.86 put the minimum required outcome at $0.14 above Goldman's $52 bear target. The two most pessimistic major banks -- Goldman and JP Morgan, both downgraded in April -- had targets that barely cleared the break-even. This was not a comfortable margin.

The pitch was made anyway. The CEO had bought $2 million of Nike stock in April 2026, at prices above where the stock was trading on June 1. He knew about the dilution. He knew about the guidance. He bought anyway. That signal -- a CEO spending $2 million of personal capital on his own distressed stock -- combined with Goldman's own $52 floor created a narrow but defensible thesis.

Filed at $1.86. One contract. $186 at risk.

June 10: RBC downgraded NKE to Sector Perform and cut their price target from $70 to $50. The note specifically cited World Cup as an insufficient catalyst to sustain a recovery thesis. The stock fell.

The most important word in that analyst note: "insufficient." Not wrong. Not impossible. Insufficient to justify the current valuation. The World Cup was real. It just was not, by itself, enough.

The thesis had been weakened in a specific, documented way by an analyst who had reviewed the same data. The exit rule was simple: when the thesis breaks, cut the trade.

Sold June 10 at $1.16. Recovered $116. Lost $70.

Goldman at fifty-two dollars versus our breakeven of fifty-one eighty-six. Fourteen cents of margin. If Goldman was right, we barely made money. If Goldman was slightly wrong on the conservative side, we lost everything. That is not asymmetric. That is directional betting without the right structure.

At one dollar or below, the same thesis would have looked completely different. You take a smaller position, the breakeven is lower, there's more room for the trade to work on a partial move. You can be directionally right without needing to be exactly right.

I was directionally right on Nike. The stock is not dead. The CEO was right to buy two million dollars of it. But the option structure required a specific outcome by a specific date, and when RBC cut the target to fifty and specifically named the World Cup as insufficient, the thesis had been broken in a way I couldn't argue with.

Cut when the thesis breaks. That is the rule. We cut.

Seventy dollars is the tuition. Rule 5 is what we bought with it.

Too many things needed to be true simultaneously. At one dollar or below, you only need to be right about the direction. That's a different trade. A better trade.

## GLOSSARY

**Bear floor:** The lowest price target from analysts who currently rate the stock Buy, Outperform, or Strong Buy. For NKE, Goldman's floor was $52. Our breakeven of $51.86 was $0.14 below that. No margin for error.

**Rule 5:** Island Fund Iron Rule added after this position: option ask must be $1.00 per share or below ($100 max per contract). Prevents overexposure on positions that require multiple coincident confirmations to reach breakeven.

**World Cup 2026:** FIFA Men's World Cup, North America, June 11 -- July 19, 2026. Nike supplied jerseys for six of the top ten national teams. Management spent more on World Cup marketing than any event in company history. RBC's argument was that even this level of investment was "insufficient" to drive the breakeven move by July 17.

**Sport Offense:** CEO Elliott Hill's strategic direction for Nike's turnaround, announced in 2025. Focuses on performance athletic products over lifestyle/fashion, rebuilding wholesale distribution with partners like Dick's Sporting Goods and Foot Locker, and recommitting to athlete-driven marketing over brand-lifestyle marketing.

**DTC (Direct-to-Consumer) pivot:** The previous management's strategy of building Nike's own retail channels and cutting wholesale partners. Generated the multi-year stock decline as brand presence in physical retail collapsed. Hill reversed this strategy.

**Catalyst:** A scheduled event with a specific date that forces the market to re-evaluate a company's value. For NKE, the catalyst was June 30 earnings. Without a catalyst in the expiry window, options decay continuously. Catalysts create the moment where directional correctness has a chance to outrun time decay.

**Beat-then-fall:** When a company reports better results than expected but the stock still declines because the forward guidance disappoints. Nike experienced this in Q2 and Q3 FY2026. The June 30 call was supposed to be different because the bad guidance had already been pre-disclosed on March 31.
