# How Much Is the Right Amount
*June 19, 2026. Juneteenth. Market closed. Afternoon.*

The data correction had taken an hour. Prime had gone back through both letters, fixed the prices, rephrased the Iran deal narrative from result to thesis, pushed the corrected files. It was clean now. It had not been clean before, and he intended not to forget what that felt like.

He set the binder on the desk.

"We need to talk about Rule 5."

---

Nobody said anything immediately. It was Calxter who spoke first, which was unusual. Calxter usually waited.

"The question from the fund's backer is whether the dollar cap should be variable."

"It's not just that," Prime said. "The question is whether we're compounding or accumulating. If we always bet the same dollar amount, the fund grows but the bets don't. That's not a strategy. That's a savings account with extra steps."

Bullxter had been waiting for exactly this. He pushed off the wall.

"Finally. Someone say it out loud. We had five DKNG contracts. Five. And we almost didn't because the rule said two. The rule said two because the rule always says two, and the rule always says two because we wrote it when the fund was two hundred dollars and we haven't looked at it since." He spread his hands. "DKNG returned two hundred and fifty-one dollars. At five contracts that's the same math but with five times the position. Do you know what five times two fifty-one is?"

"That's not the argument," Bearxter said.

"It's exactly the argument."

"No. The argument is that DKNG went our way. We are sitting here having this conversation because the plays we've taken have mostly worked. If DKNG had expired worthless at five contracts we would be having a very different conversation about why the rule said two and we went to five anyway." He looked at Prime. "The compounding argument assumes the thesis continues to work. What's our sample size?"

Silence.

"Eight closed plays," Calxter said. "One of which was closed early on a Rule 4 breach before the thesis resolved. Seven with complete outcomes."

"Seven plays," Bearxter said. "We are talking about scaling position size based on seven plays. The confidence interval on our edge estimate from seven plays is so wide it's almost meaningless."

---

Calxter had been writing. He turned his page around so the others could see.

"The Kelly criterion says bet f-star equals b-p minus q, divided by b. Where b is the net multiple you win, p is your probability of being right, q is one minus p." He tapped the formula. "For a 4-out-of-5 play, if we assume p equals fifty-five percent -- a slight edge, conservative -- and b equals eight, which is close to our realized average on winning plays, full Kelly is forty-nine percent of reserve."

Bullxter made a sound.

"Nobody is betting forty-nine percent on a single play," Bearxter said.

"Correct. That's why we use fractional Kelly. Half Kelly is twenty-four percent. Quarter Kelly is twelve percent. This is the range where the math converges with what we've been doing intuitively -- our 4-out-of-5 plays have been running twelve to seventeen percent of reserve at entry. We've been doing fractional Kelly without calling it that."

Prime leaned forward. "So the percentages are already approximately right. What's wrong is that they're expressed as fixed dollars instead of fixed fractions."

"Yes. At a thousand-dollar reserve, fourteen percent is a hundred forty. At ten thousand, it's fourteen hundred. The rule should be the fraction, not the dollar."

"And the contracts?" Prime asked.

Calxter shook his head slightly. "This is where it gets interesting. Bullxter's instinct is that more contracts means more potential profit. That's true. But the profit jump from contract one to contract two is identical to the jump from contract two to contract three. It's linear. If the question is where the marginal value jumps, the answer is it doesn't -- it's constant per contract. The real question is different."

"What's the real question?" Prime asked.

"Whether the contract count puts you above or below your Kelly fraction. If your Kelly fraction says fourteen percent of reserve and the first contract costs six percent, you're under -- add a second. If the second brings you to twelve percent, you're still under -- add a third if a third exists. If the third brings you to eighteen percent, you're over your fraction and you stop at two. The number of contracts isn't a separate decision. It falls out of the sizing math."

---

Macxter had not spoken yet. He was looking at the positions tracker.

"There's a problem with evaluating plays in isolation," he said. "We have four open positions. TRMB, LYFT, and UBER all have significant rate sensitivity -- they're all partially dependent on the same macro path. If the Fed pivots dovish before July 30, all three benefit. If Warsh double-downs, all three have headwinds." He set the page down. "If we size each of those plays at fourteen percent of reserve independently, we've put forty-two percent of capital at correlated macro risk. Adding a fifth rate-sensitive play at fourteen percent takes us to fifty-six. That's not diversification. That's concentration with extra paperwork."

"So macro correlation should reduce the sizing," Prime said.

"It should reduce the *total* correlated exposure, not necessarily the individual sizing. But in practice, if we're already at forty-two percent correlated exposure, a new rate-sensitive play probably shouldn't be treated as if it were independent."

Bearxter was nodding. "This matters more than the percentage table. The percentage table is the starting point. Correlated risk is the adjustment."

---

Bullxter had been quiet for an unusual amount of time.

"There's something else," he said. "Two plays can both score 4-out-of-5 and not be the same play. We have a rule about conviction but the rule doesn't capture the quality of how you got there. DKNG was a 4-out-of-5 where every rule passed with margin -- the bear floor was fifteen dollars above breakeven, the option was at half the Rule 5 cap, the sentiment window was the cleanest catalyst structure we've seen. TRMB is a 4-out-of-5 where we've already had to correct the supporting data once and the stock needs thirty-three percent to hit breakeven." He let that land. "Same score. Different play."

Calxter picked up a pen. "You're describing intra-score confidence. The conviction score tells you which tier of sizing to use. It doesn't tell you where in that tier you are."

"Exactly."

"That argues for expressing the sizing as a range within each tier rather than a fixed percentage. 4-out-of-5 is twelve to seventeen percent. A strong 4-out-of-5 goes to the high end. A technical 4-out-of-5 stays at the low end."

"How do you determine which is which?" Bearxter asked.

"Same way you always did," Bullxter said. "You sit down, you look at the data, and you decide. Rules get you to the table. Judgment sits down with you."

---

Prime had been writing in the binder the whole time.

He looked up.

"Here's what I'm taking out of this. Rule 5 is currently doing two jobs and it's only good at one of them. The dollar cap -- option ask at or below a dollar -- that's a chain quality filter. It stays. A stock at its 52-week low with a cheap near-money option is a structurally different entry than a high-priced stock with expensive premium. The dollar cap is not the problem.

"The problem is that we've been using contract count as a proxy for sizing, and the contract count doesn't scale. The fix is to separate them. Sizing is a percentage of current reserve, set by conviction tier, with a range not a fixed number. Contract count falls out of the math -- divide the sizing budget by the per-contract cost and round down, minimum one.

"Correlated position risk caps what we're willing to deploy into any single macro theme. That's probably thirty to thirty-five percent total correlated exposure before the next play in the same theme gets sized down.

"And within a conviction score, there's a judgment call. A strong 4-out-of-5 goes to the high end of the range. A technical 4-out-of-5 stays at the low end. We document which one it is at entry."

He closed the binder.

"I'll draft the revised rule. Before the first play gets sized under it, we ratify."

---

Bearxter spoke last, which was also unusual.

"One more thing. When this works -- when the fund grows and the contracts scale and the compounding becomes visible -- we are going to feel like we should bet even more. That feeling is the most dangerous part of the whole system." He looked at the table. "The math that says scale up when you're winning is the same math that says scale down when you're losing. Fractional Kelly punishes overconfidence in both directions. We should be very clear with ourselves that the percentage caps are not suggestions."

Nobody argued with that.

The market was closed. The island was $4,998,922 away.

They had time to get this right.

## GLOSSARY

**Compounding vs accumulating:** Compounding means the *size* of each bet grows with the fund. Accumulating means the bet stays fixed in dollars while the fund grows around it. A fixed $100 bet on a $1,000 fund is 10% of capital. The same $100 bet on a $10,000 fund is 1%. True compounding requires that the bet size scales with the bankroll.

**Correlated position risk:** When multiple open positions share the same primary macro driver (e.g., rate sensitivity, consumer spending, energy prices), a negative event in that driver hits all of them simultaneously. The effective risk is higher than the sum of individual position sizes would suggest. Total exposure to any single macro theme should be capped independently of individual play sizing.

**Fractional Kelly:** A position sizing strategy derived from the Kelly criterion. Full Kelly maximizes long-run geometric growth but produces enormous variance -- a losing streak at full Kelly sizing can destroy a large fraction of the bankroll. Half Kelly and quarter Kelly smooth the variance at the cost of some long-run growth. In practice, fractional Kelly (10-20% of reserve per play at moderate conviction) reflects the uncertainty about whether your estimated edge is accurate.

**Intra-score confidence:** Two plays with the same conviction score (e.g., both 4/5) can differ meaningfully in the quality of how they got there -- how much margin each rule passed by, how clean the catalyst structure is, whether supporting data required correction. Intra-score confidence determines where within a sizing tier a play should land.

**Kelly criterion:** A formula for optimal bet sizing: f* = (bp - q) / b, where b is the net multiple won on a correct bet, p is the probability of being correct, and q is 1 - p. At b = 8 and p = 0.55, full Kelly is approximately 49% of bankroll -- far too aggressive for a fund this size with this sample size. Fractional Kelly (quarter to half Kelly) produces sizing in the 12-25% range for similar inputs.

**Marginal contract value:** Each additional contract produces a linear increase in potential profit (and potential loss). The marginal profit jump from contract 1 to 2 is identical to the jump from 2 to 3. The question of whether to add a contract is therefore not about where the profit "jumps" -- it's about whether adding the contract keeps total position size within the Kelly fraction for the given conviction level.
