# The Other Side of the Market
*June 18, 2026. Evening. After the UBER and LYFT entries. After MCD came back a pass.*

The room was quieter than it had been all day.

Prime had the binder open to the positions page. Four plays live. $614 in reserve. The Iran deal had flipped the morning from disaster to opportunity inside of ten minutes, and somewhere in the middle of all of it, a question had surfaced that nobody had answered yet.

He closed the binder.

"We need to talk about puts."

---

Bullxter had been leaning against the wall. He stood up straight.

"Finally."

"Don't," Bearxter said.

"Don't what? This is a real question. We passed on MCD today because the only Rule 5 instrument was the $330C and the chain couldn't support it. But MCD is at $279 and falling. It's been falling. If we had a puts system, MCD is not a pass. MCD is an entry." He let that sit. "How many plays have we walked away from because we couldn't find a call that worked, when the put was sitting right there?"

"Zero," Bearxter said. "Because we don't have a puts system. You can't count plays you haven't screened."

"That's exactly my point."

---

Prime looked at Calxter.

Calxter had not moved. He was looking at a page he'd apparently been filling in while everyone else was talking.

"The question is a portfolio question before it's a mechanics question," he said. "If we assume the market distributes roughly symmetrically -- stocks at 52-week lows offset by stocks at 52-week highs, roughly similar distributions of catalyst quality -- then a calls-only screen is excluding approximately half the available opportunity set by construction."

"Half," Bullxter said.

"Approximately. The actual number depends on market conditions. In a bull market, the distribution tilts toward calls. In a bear market or a sector rotation, puts become the higher-expected-value side." He turned the page. "Today is a reasonable example. Iranian crude re-enters the market. Energy names near 52-week highs. If we had been screening energy puts this morning, we would have been looking at a different set of plays."

Macxter spoke from the window. He'd been watching the screen since before the meeting started.

"XOM was at ninety-three percent of its 52-week range at open. CVX at eighty-nine percent. OXY at eighty-seven. All three have Q2 earnings inside an August 21 window." He paused. "I'm not saying they were good plays. I'm saying we didn't look."

---

Bearxter turned to face the room.

"Let me be precise about what building a puts system actually requires. Because Bullxter is going to say 'just invert the rules' and I want to explain why that is not a complete answer."

Bullxter opened his mouth.

"I know," Bearxter said. "Let me finish."

He pulled Prime's chair out and sat in it. He did not usually sit.

"Rule 1 for calls: stock in the bottom 20-25% of its 52-week range. Inverted for puts: stock in the top 20-25% of its range. That part is clean. XOM, CVX, OXY passed this morning. Fine."

"Rule 2: earnings catalyst before expiry. Same for puts. No change."

"Rule 3: near-zero Sell ratings. This one fully inverts. For calls we want consensus Buy because we need the analyst floor to hold the stock up. For puts we want the opposite -- the analyst community should be turning, or already turned. Multiple Sell ratings. No dominant Buy consensus. The reason is symmetric: if fifteen analysts rate the stock a Buy with an average target forty dollars above current price, you need those analysts to be wrong for the put to work. That is a structural headwind. For puts, you want the analysts already agreeing the stock is going down."

He let Bullxter say it.

"MCD has one Sell rating and thirty-seven Buys," Bullxter said. "So MCD fails inverted Rule 3."

"Correct. MCD was never a puts play under a proper system either. But there are names that would pass."

---

"Rule 4," Bearxter continued. "This is where it gets structural and where 'just invert it' breaks down."

"For calls: the bear floor -- the lowest Buy analyst target -- must be above the call breakeven. This protects us from entering a position where even the most pessimistic bull thinks the stock is going to land below where we need it."

"For puts, the equivalent is a bull ceiling. The highest price target among Sell-rated analysts must be below the put breakeven. If you buy a $50 put at $0.80, your breakeven is $49.20. The most optimistic bear on the Street -- the least convinced Sell rating -- must still think the stock is heading below $49.20. If the most bullish Sell analyst has a target of $52, the Sell thesis doesn't even reach your breakeven."

Calxter was already writing.

"The bull ceiling concept requires that there are Sell ratings to build a ceiling from," Bearxter said. "Which means Rule 3 inverted is not just a filter -- it is the prerequisite for Rule 4 inverted to exist at all. No Sell ratings means no bull ceiling means Rule 4 cannot pass. The rules are load-bearing in order."

The room was quiet.

"Rule 5 is the same," he said. "Option ask at or below $1.00. No change."

---

Prime had been listening with the binder closed on his knee.

"Calxter. If we built this system and ran it alongside the calls screen -- same weekly pass/fail discipline, separate column in the watch file -- what does that cost us?"

Calxter set his pen flat on the page.

"Research time, primarily. The screen itself is fast -- the criteria are defined, the data sources are the same. The cost is attention and the risk of dilution." He picked the pen back up. "Right now we run one system and we run it well. We have twelve position logs. We have learned the rules by applying them. Adding a second system doubles the decision surface without doubling the available capital. The question is whether the opportunity set we're missing is worth the operational cost of maintaining the discipline on two separate screens simultaneously."

"What's your read?" Prime asked.

"I don't have one. That's an opinion question. I gave you the parameters."

---

Bullxter crossed his arms.

"Here's what I think. The reason we built the Iron Rules was so that the decision is never emotional. We screen, we score, we enter or we pass. The calls system works because we don't deviate from it. A puts system built with the same rigor would have the same property. We would not be guessing. We would be screening."

"The discipline is the point," Bearxter said.

"Right. So the question is not whether puts are risky. Options are risky. We know that. The question is whether we can build rules that make the decision disciplined. And you just described the rules." He looked at Bearxter. "You described them clearly enough that Calxter was writing them down."

Bearxter did not answer immediately.

"I described the structure. I did not say it was ready to trade."

"What's missing?"

Bearxter was quiet for a moment.

"Back-testing. We have twelve trades of call history. I know how the Iron Rules perform because I have watched them perform. I do not know how inverted Iron Rules perform because we have never run them. Before the first puts entry, I want to know: how would these rules have scored the plays we passed on in the last three weeks? Not whether they would have made money -- that's hindsight. Whether the rules would have selected them correctly and excluded the ones that didn't fit."

---

Macxter had not moved from the window.

"The Iran deal today is the argument for building the system. Not for entering tomorrow. For building the system." He said it without turning around. "There were energy names this morning where the macro thesis was clear, the stocks were near 52-week highs, Q2 earnings land inside August 21, and the catalyst had just fired. We didn't have the infrastructure to evaluate them. We passed by default -- not because we screened and declined, but because we didn't have a screen."

"Passing by default is the same outcome as passing on the merits," Bearxter said.

"No," Macxter said. "It's not. Passing on the merits means we know why. Passing by default means we might have missed the highest-conviction play of the week and we'll never know."

---

Prime stood up.

"Here is where we land."

He opened the binder to a clean page. He uncapped the pen.

"Bearxter described the inverted rules clearly enough to write down. We write them down. Not next week -- tonight. Five rules. Same structure as the calls rules: numbered, specific, no ambiguity about pass or fail."

"Then we back-test them against our existing pass list. Every name in passes.md that we declined on -- we run it through the puts screen and see if any of them would have passed on the other side. If the screen identifies real plays we missed, that is the confirmation the system has value. If it identifies nothing, that is also useful information."

"We do not enter a puts play until we have run the screen at least once and seen what it catches. No exceptions."

He wrote at the top of the page: *INVERTED IRON RULES -- DRAFT.*

Bullxter looked at it. He did not say anything, which was unusual.

"One more thing," Prime said. "The puts system does not replace the calls system. It runs alongside it. Separate column in the watch file. Separate conviction scoring. If a week produces two clean call plays and one clean put play, we have three plays to compare. Capital still goes to the highest conviction play that passes all rules. The system does not change. The screen expands."

---

Bearxter looked at what Prime had written.

"There is one more structural difference I want on the page."

"Say it."

"For calls, the position needs the stock to move up and stay above the breakeven by expiry. Time works against us -- theta decay. For puts, the structure is the same, but the psychology is different. A stock falling triggers fear. Fear triggers volume. Volume is not always rational. Puts can move faster than calls in the same absolute distance because the market responds to downside asymmetrically."

"That sounds like a Bullxter argument," Prime said.

"It is. I am stating it accurately because it should be in the rules document. The puts system may identify higher-velocity plays than the calls system. That is not an argument against it. It is an argument for understanding exactly what we are entering before we enter it."

Bullxter looked at Bearxter with an expression that was somewhere between surprised and respectful.

"Write it down," Bearxter said. Not to Bullxter. To Prime.

Prime wrote it down.

---

The binder had four pages on the new section by the time they stopped.

The inverted rules were drafted. The back-test protocol was drafted. The column header for the watch file was agreed on. The first puts screen would run this weekend -- same sources, same process, different direction.

Prime capped the pen.

"The question was whether we're limiting our upside by not screening puts. The answer is: probably, yes. We've been operating with half the market filtered out by omission. The answer to that is not to jump in. The answer is to build the rules and run them before we touch a single instrument."

He closed the binder.

"Calxter -- what is the expected value of building a system correctly before using it versus building it while using it?"

Calxter did not look up.

"The expected value is not the relevant calculation. The variance is. Building it while using it produces an unknown error rate on real capital. Building it correctly first produces a known error rate before real capital is at risk."

"Right."

Bullxter sat back down.

"This weekend," he said.

"This weekend," Prime said.

---

*The binder went back on the desk.*

*Four tabs.*

*There would be five.*
