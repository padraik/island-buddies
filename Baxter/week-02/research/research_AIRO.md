## GLOSSARY

*Every acronym and referenced event in this file -- plain English.*

**Avionics:** Electronics and software systems for aircraft navigation, communication, and monitoring. AIRO's Avionics segment sells flight displays, GPS/GNSS sensors, and connected panels to military aircraft, drones, and eVTOL manufacturers. These are hardware and software products with recurring upgrade cycles.

**Blue UAS (Unmanned Aerial Systems) certification:** A US government program that evaluates and approves specific drone models for federal procurement. Companies with Blue UAS certification are placed on an approved supplier list that government agencies are required to use when purchasing drones. Receiving certification is equivalent to unlocking a mandatory customer base for government drone sales.

**Backlog:** Signed contracts or purchase orders for which revenue has not yet been recognized. AIRO's $150M drone segment backlog represents orders they have received that will be fulfilled and billed in future quarters. It is the most reliable forward indicator of revenue for their primary growth segment.

**BTIG:** A mid-size investment bank with active coverage of defense and technology companies. Their downgrade of AIRO from Strong Buy to Hold in May 2026 (after Q1 earnings) is the most recent analyst action and the most cautious signal from the covering analysts.

**eVTOL (Electric Vertical Takeoff and Landing):** A category of aircraft that uses electric motors to take off and land vertically -- the technology behind air taxis. AIRO has an Electric Air Mobility segment developing eVTOL products. The sector is facing regulatory delays and corporate governance disputes in 2026 that have created headwinds.

**FCF (Free Cash Flow):** Cash generated from operations minus capital expenditures. AIRO's FCF was +$20.7M in FY2024 and turned to -$35.5M in FY2025 -- a $56M swing. Negative FCF means the company is spending more cash than it generates, requiring either new debt or new equity issuance to continue operating.

**Forward PE:** Price-to-earnings ratio using expected future earnings. AIRO has no current PE (it's losing money). Calxter's model shows a forward PE of 6.75x in FY2028 if the company reaches $1.37 in EPS as analysts project. At 15x (a conservative defense peer multiple), that implies a $20.55 stock price. At 20x, $27.40.

**Gross margin:** Revenue minus cost of goods sold, divided by revenue. AIRO's gross margin declined from 67% (FY2024) to 56.6% (TTM) -- the product mix shift management referenced. Higher-margin software and IP licensing contracts were replaced by lower-margin hardware and service contracts, compressing the amount of each revenue dollar that flows to profit.

**NDAA (National Defense Authorization Act) compliance:** US defense procurement law requires that certain military equipment be sourced from American-made or allied-nation products. NDAA-compliant drones are those that meet these sourcing requirements. This is a prerequisite for selling to the US military and most federal agencies.

**Non-operating income:** Income from sources other than the company's core business operations. AIRO shows $38-42M in non-operating income despite operating losses of similar magnitude. This is almost certainly from warrant fair-value adjustments or derivative gains -- non-cash accounting entries that inflate reported income without generating real money. Do not use this to assess underlying business health.

**Product mix:** The combination of different products and services a company sells in a given period. When AIRO says revenue and margins declined due to "product mix," it means the proportion of high-margin contracts (typically software, IP licensing, specialized drone systems) fell relative to lower-margin work (commodity hardware, basic services). This can be a temporary timing issue or a structural shift.

**SG&A (Selling, General & Administrative expenses):** The overhead costs of running a company -- salaries, marketing, rent, legal, executive compensation. AIRO's SG&A jumped from $24.6M to $65.3M in one year while revenue grew only 4.6%. This suggests they hired aggressively in anticipation of growth that didn't materialize. For the business to be healthy, either revenue must grow to $150M+ or SG&A must be reduced significantly.

**Share dilution:** When a company issues new shares, existing shareholders own a smaller percentage of the company. AIRO's shares outstanding went from 16.4M to 31.4M -- nearly doubling. If business value stayed constant, the per-share value would be cut roughly in half. Combined with slowing revenue growth, this explains most of the stock's decline from $39 to $9.

**52-week range:** The highest and lowest prices at which a stock traded over the past year. AIRO's range of $5.71-$39.07 shows extreme volatility. At $9.23, the stock is near its annual low -- 10% of the way from bottom to top. This is the "beaten down" signal our framework looks for, but context matters: the high of $39.07 was reached on the original growth story before dilution and deceleration, not during a period of comparable fundamentals.
