## THE THESIS

Trimble has spent five years selling hardware businesses and turning into a software company. The agriculture business (sold to CNH in 2023). The civil infrastructure segment (partial sale to Caterpillar in 2023). What is left is the highest-margin, most defensible part of the business: construction workflows, geospatial software, and fleet telematics -- all transitioning to subscription ARR.

Q1 2026 showed this transition is working. EPS of $0.42, up from $0.27 in Q1 2025. Revenue $939.9M, up 12% year-over-year. Management guided Q2 at $938M-$963M and $0.80 EPS. These are not distressed numbers. These are the numbers of a company executing a difficult transition successfully.

The stock has not noticed. It has gone from $87.50 to $50.11 while earnings per share went up 56%.

July 30 is when the market gets a second data point. If Q2 confirms Q1 -- consistent ARR growth, margins holding, subscription mix increasing -- there is no fundamental argument for a $50 stock on a company with $93.50 average analyst consensus. The re-rating begins.

Why the stock is down despite strong earnings: Trimble's remaining businesses (construction and transportation) are tied to real-world capital spending. Higher interest rates slow construction starts. Supply chain normalization reduces fleet management urgency. The market has priced in a macro slowdown that has not yet shown up in the financials.

If Warsh signals any rate relief at the June 16-17 FOMC, rate-sensitive construction tech companies re-rate immediately. TRMB is one of the most direct beneficiaries of that signal.

**ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue):** The predictable subscription revenue Trimble collects from customers under multi-year software contracts. Trimble's strategy is converting its historically hardware-heavy business to software subscriptions. ARR growth is the primary metric analysts use to value Trimble's software transition progress.

**Construction tech:** Software and hardware tools used on job sites and in project management offices. Trimble's Viewpoint (ERP), SketchUp (design), and ProjectSight (field management) platforms serve general contractors and specialty subcontractors. Tied to construction starts and capital investment cycles.

**Fleet telematics:** GPS tracking and workflow software for transportation and logistics fleets. Trimble's transportation segment monitors truck location, driver behavior, and fuel efficiency. Customer contracts are multi-year and largely recurring.

**Bear floor:** The lowest price target held by any analyst who still recommends buying the stock. For TRMB, Wells Fargo analyst Jerry Revich holds the most pessimistic Buy-rated target at $79. The Aug21 $65C breakeven of $65.65 sits $13.35 below this floor -- meaning even the most bearish bull on Wall Street sees 20.3% upside above the trade's breakeven.

**52-week range position:** How far the current price sits above the 52-week low, expressed as a percentage of the full range. TRMB at 1.8% is essentially at the floor of its annual trading range -- price has not been lower in 52 weeks.

**FOMC rate sensitivity:** Trimble's construction customers make multi-year capital commitments. When interest rates fall, the NPV of those projects improves and construction starts accelerate. When rates rise, projects get deferred. The June 16-17 FOMC meeting is a near-term macro catalyst that could move TRMB before July 30 earnings.
