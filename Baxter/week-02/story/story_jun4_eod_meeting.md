# The End-of-Day Meeting
*June 4, 1996. One hour to close.*

The bedroom smells like carpet and old pizza and the particular staleness of a room where two people have been staring at a computer screen for six hours. The monitor is still on. The Robinhood app is open. The binder is on the desk.

Prime didn't tap it twice this time. He just opened it.

---

"One hour to close," Prime said. "Six positions. Let's go through them in order of how much time I want to spend."

Bearxter was already sitting. He'd been sitting before Prime opened the binder.

Bullxter was standing. He hadn't sat down since the DSGX chart went vertical and then fell off a cliff.

Calxter had his yellow legal pad. He'd been writing since before the meeting started.

Macxter was by the window.

---

**"DSGX."**

Prime put his finger on the line. Forty-five dollars. Down ninety-eight percent. Bid zero.

Bullxter said it first, which surprised everyone. "Done. Gap and fade. We lose the forty-five and we learn the lesson." He didn't say anything else. He sat down.

Bearxter looked at him for a moment. Then: "Agreed. The bid is zero. There's nothing to discuss." He made a small mark on his page.

Calxter: "One to three percent probability of expiring with any value. Expected value on the remaining position is under two dollars. The transaction cost of selling exceeds the recovery. Let it expire."

Macxter, from the window: "I'm watching whether any trade normalization announcement comes before June 18. G7 trade track this week. If a deal gets announced, the thesis compresses faster. Doesn't change the outcome on this contract."

Prime closed the DSGX tab. "Next."

---

**"CHWY."**

Bullxter: "I'll say it before Bearxter does. This is ugly. Three eighteen below the strike. Six days. Eighteen percent needed. I've looked at it." He paused. "I think it's probably a loss."

The room was quiet for a second. That was not a sentence Bullxter normally produced.

Bearxter: "No positive expected value scenario I can construct. The only question is whether thirty-seven dollars back is worth more than holding a lottery ticket." He let that sit. "I'd take the thirty-seven."

Calxter: "The market and I agree the position has deteriorated. I'm pricing this at twelve to eighteen percent probability of finishing in the money. We paid fifty-six cents. The current ask of thirty-seven cents reflects that deterioration accurately. One soft parameter: CHWY has historically moved ten to fifteen percent on earnings. Getting to twenty-four requires the upper tail of that distribution."

Macxter: "CPI drops June 10. Same day as CHWY earnings. If CPI runs hot, consumer spending narrative compresses regardless of the actual Chewy numbers. Also: the May 12 tariff reduction on consumer goods is constructive for Chewy's cost structure. One positive signal, one complicating signal. Same morning."

Prime looked at Bearxter. "Your recommendation is to sell."

"Thirty-seven cents is real money," Bearxter said. "The alternative is zero."

Prime wrote something down. "We'll let it ride to the open June 10. If the stock doesn't show pre-market movement before 9:15, we sell at market. We don't hold through a print that needs eighteen percent."

Bullxter didn't argue.

---

**"NKE."**

Bullxter was back on his feet. "Thesis is intact. I don't care what the chart looks like. Twenty-six days, first full year from Elliott Hill, World Cup data live on the June 30 call. The market is pricing maybe twenty-five percent chance we're right. I think the market is wrong."

Calxter: "I have it at eight to fourteen percent probability. The market is more optimistic than my model. The parameter I can't model is the World Cup revenue pull-forward and how much of it is already in the forty-three-sixty stock. That input is soft."

Bearxter: "I'll stay in. The thesis is intact. One condition â€” I want no negative pre-announcements before June 20. If one comes, the thesis is broken early and we exit. We don't wait for June 30 to find out something that was knowable in June 15."

Macxter, still by the window: "There's a filing from March. A member of the House Ways and Means Committee disclosed NKE purchases prior to the tariff exemption announcement on footwear imports. The exemption was subsequently extended. I'm not assigning causality. I'm noting the administration has demonstrated favorable posture toward Nike's supply chain specifically." A pause. "And the current administration has been publicly supportive of the World Cup. No adverse signals in the next twenty-six days."

Bullxter: "See? Even Macxter likes it."

Bearxter: "That's not what he said."

Macxter said nothing.

Prime: "We hold. June 20 is the watch date. If there's a pre-announcement, we reassess immediately."

---

**"ABT."**

Prime looked around the room. Nobody said anything combative. That had never happened before.

Bullxter: "CEO bought two million dollars of stock at a hundred and seven. Stock is at ninety. He's sixteen dollars underwater on his own purchase. He either knows something or he's the worst insider trader in history. I know which way I'm betting."

Bearxter: "No objection. Cleanest insider signal in the fund. Forty-two days. The thesis hasn't moved."

Calxter: "Twenty to twenty-eight percent probability of expiring in the money under standard assumptions. Near fair value with positive skew from the earnings catalyst. The parameter carrying the most weight is whether the first full Exact Sciences quarter actually delivers the revenue recognition the thesis assumes. I cannot model that. But the structure is the cleanest in the book."

Macxter: "Healthcare deregulatory posture from the current administration is constructive for device and diagnostics approvals. No adverse signals. No disclosed positions relevant to ABT. Clean."

Prime wrote *approved* next to ABT and meant something different by it than usual â€” not a new approval, a confirmation that the thesis had survived another day of scrutiny.

---

**"HITI."**

Bearxter: "I have a question about the analyst target. Five sixty-seven on a two thirty-eight stock is a hundred and thirty-eight percent premium. Either that reflects genuine mispricing or it's a small-cap analyst working from an optimistic model with no price discipline. I want to understand which. I'm watching whether volume builds into June 15. If the stock goes quiet into earnings, I want to know why."

Calxter: "The position is flat relative to entry. That's the most unusual thing about it. The market is not pricing in a large move. That could be an opportunity or it could be information. I don't have historical volatility data for HITI around earnings. The model is soft on this input. Binary outcome. Fairly priced at current levels."

Bullxter: "Four contracts at twenty-five cents. Hundred bucks at risk. If HITI runs on the print, that's where the big number comes from. This is our highest-leverage position. I want to be in it."

Macxter: "Canadian regulatory environment. The current U.S. administration can't help or hurt this one directly. That's insulation. One thing I'm watching: the Senate schedule on the SAFER Banking Act. No markup confirmed. If one gets scheduled before June 15 it reprices the whole sector, direction uncertain."

Prime: "Eleven days. We hold."

---

**"MDT."**

Bullxter: "Seventy-four percent. The question I want answered is whether we ride to ninety-five like we planned or take the forty now and reload into something with a catalyst."

Bearxter, flat: "When it hits ninety-five, we sell. That's not a question. The Iron Rules exist precisely for this moment. We set the exit before we were in the trade. We honor it."

Calxter: "At current levels, the probability of reaching ninety-five before July 17 is four to eight percent. That is low. We are currently holding a forty-dollar unrealized gain on a fifty-four dollar position. The expected value of holding versus realizing depends on assumptions about underlying drift that I cannot make without a catalyst. This is the softest parameter in the book."

Prime: "Calxter, what's the expected value of holding versus selling at current ask?"

Calxter wrote something. Looked up. "Holding: four to eight percent probability of a ninety-five-plus outcome â€” option worth ten dollars or more â€” produces an expected value of forty to eighty cents per contract. Current gain: forty dollars. If I assign forty dollars as certain and the hold as four to eight percent of a much larger number, the sale has higher expected value under conservative assumptions. Under optimistic assumptions, they're close."

Bullxter started to say something.

Bearxter: "We set the exit at ninety-five. We don't change exits because the math is hard."

Bullxter: "I wasn't going to argue. I was going to say the same thing."

Bearxter looked at him.

"The exit plan is the exit plan," Bullxter said. "We hold to ninety-five or July 17, whichever comes first. That's what we said."

Prime: "Agreed. MDT holds. No change."

---

Macxter spoke from the window. He'd been quiet since the NKE filing.

"One structural note." He turned around. "We are fully deployed. Zero reserve. Two positions expire June 18 â€” CHWY and DSGX. If there is a macro shock in the next two weeks, we have no capital to move. That's not a political signal. That's a capital structure observation."

The room was quiet.

Prime wrote it at the bottom of the page. Then he closed the binder.

"Fourteen days to the first expiry. We watch CHWY into June 10. We watch NKE for pre-announcements. We hold everything else to plan."

He tapped the binder once. Not twice â€” once. The meeting was already over.

Outside the window, it was still afternoon.

The island was still $4,999,500 away.
