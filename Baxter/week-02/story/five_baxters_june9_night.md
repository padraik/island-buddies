# The Night Before
*June 9, 1996. Monday night. The car ride is over.*

The bedroom was quiet. The monitor was on. The Robinhood screen showed a number that had not existed this morning: **+218%.**

DKNG had crossed the strike at some point in the afternoon and had not come back down.

Prime called the meeting at nine o'clock, which was late for a school night and early for what needed to be discussed. Four Baxters arranged themselves around the room. Calxter was already writing.

---

Bullxter had been standing near the desk for most of the last hour. He had not sat down.

"I want to say something," he said.

"You've said it eleven times," Bearxter said.

"I want to say it a twelfth."

"Say it."

"We are *in the money.*" He spread his hands. "The stock is above the strike. Our contract has real value. Not time value. Not hope. Actual dollars baked in. We paid forty-nine cents per share. The option is worth a dollar fifty-six. The World Cup has not played a single match. I want to acknowledge this."

"Acknowledged," Prime said. "Sit down."

Bullxter sat down. He lasted about four seconds and then stood back up.

---

Bearxter spoke to the room without preamble.

"The number tomorrow is four-point-two."

"Consensus," Calxter said, without looking up.

"Consensus. Year-over-year CPI for May. April was three-point-eight. The jump is being driven by energy prices. The Iran situation." Bearxter paused. "The market has known this number was coming for weeks. The question is not whether four-point-two is bad. The question is whether May comes in above four-point-two."

"Walk through it," Prime said.

---

Calxter set his pencil down. He had three lines on the page.

"Three scenarios. First: four-point-two or below. Market shrugs. Risk assets hold. The World Cup narrative has no competition. We do nothing and let the limit sell work." He looked at the page. "Second: above four-point-two, materially. Call it four-four or higher. Risk assets reprice at open. DKNG is not immune. In that scenario, the question becomes whether the option holds the floor."

"The floor is a dollar," Bullxter said.

"The floor is a dollar," Calxter confirmed. "Third scenario: somewhere between four-two and four-four. A number that is technically a miss but not a disaster. In that case, you watch the option price at open and you do not act unless it breaks toward the floor."

Silence.

"That's the whole decision tree," Prime said.

"That's the whole decision tree."

---

Macxter had been at the window with his notebook. He turned around.

"The reason four-point-two is already priced in: the administration struck Iran in late February. Iran closed the Strait of Hormuz. Twenty million barrels a day stopped moving. Energy prices spiked through March, April, and into May. The street has been modeling this into the May print for two months." He flipped a page. "What the street has not modeled is a number that comes in *below* four-two. If May inflation tracked lower than expected, it would be the first time that sentence has been true since the strait closed."

"Cleveland Fed's nowcast," Bearxter said.

"Cleveland Fed nowcast is pointing toward modest improvement. It is not a guarantee. It is a signal." Macxter looked at the group. "The scenario where we wake up at six-thirty and the option has already moved up: that is the soft-print scenario. That is the one where we don't have a meeting at seven-thirty. We just watch the number go."

Bullxter made a sound.

---

"I have a question," Bullxter said. He sat down. He was being careful now. "Hot CPI. Option opens at a dollar-ten. Holding. Are we selling a dollar-ten?"

"No," Prime said immediately.

"I want to be clear why not."

"Because a dollar-ten is not the floor. A dollar-ten means the thesis is under pressure but not broken. You watch. You do not sell a dollar-ten into a bad CPI print and then watch the World Cup open eight hours later and the option run to two-fifty." Prime picked up the binder. "The floor is the floor. The floor is a dollar. If it opens below a dollar, you sell. If it opens above a dollar and holds, you hold."

Bearxter: "And if it opens at a dollar-five and is ticking down?"

"Then you use your eyes. If it crosses below a dollar you sell. You do not wait for it to touch ninety-eight cents and recover. A dollar is the line."

---

Bullxter: "What time is open?"

Macxter: "Seven-thirty our time."

Bullxter: "And CPI?"

Macxter: "Six-thirty."

Bullxter: "So there's a full hour between the number and the bell."

Calxter: "Correct. Pre-market will start reacting at six-thirty. Futures will move. By seven-thirty you have a direction. You are not making a blind decision at open."

---

Prime looked at the room.

"Here is what I want everyone to understand about tomorrow. The CPI print at six-thirty is a risk event. It is not the thesis. The thesis is the World Cup. The thesis is a billion people watching soccer and downloading DraftKings to put money on it. The CPI is a variable that can interfere with the thesis. It is not the thesis itself." He set the binder on the desk. "We held through today because the thesis was intact. Tomorrow morning the same logic applies. If six-thirty comes and goes clean, we are in the best possible position: in the money, limit sell set, World Cup opener tonight, twenty-three days on the clock."

Macxter: "The match is tomorrow night. Late."

"Right. The thesis does not activate at six-thirty in the morning. The thesis activates when the first whistle blows."

---

Bearxter had been quiet for a few minutes. He spoke slowly.

"I want to say one thing before we close."

The room waited.

"Everything we decided this morning held. The floor. The limit sell. The thesis. Patrick got in the car, we gave him the structure, and he held." He paused. "That is not guaranteed to happen again tomorrow. He is going to wake up, read four-point-two, watch the option move at open, and feel everything he felt this morning multiplied by a CPI print and the World Cup starting. The structure has to hold again." He looked at Prime. "So I want to say it plainly: if the print is clean tomorrow, do not let him celebrate early. The limit sell is not a floor. It is a ceiling. We are holding for three-fifty, not one-fifty, not two dollars. The structure holds until the structure triggers."

Prime nodded. "Agreed."

Bullxter opened his mouth.

"Don't," Bearxter said.

Bullxter closed it.

---

The meeting ended at ten-fifteen.

Calxter's decision tree was on a single page, torn from the back of the binder, on the desk near the monitor:

*CPI 6:30 AM:*
*At or below 4.2%: hold. No action. Let the thesis run.*
*Above 4.2%: phones out at 7:30. Watch the open.*
*Option below $1.00 at open: sell. Take the profit. Walk away.*
*Option above $1.00 and holding: hold. Floor is the floor, not the ceiling.*

Below that, in Macxter's handwriting:

*World Cup opener: tomorrow night. That's when the thesis activates.*

Below that, in Bullxter's handwriting, added without permission:

*we are in the money*

Nobody crossed it out.
