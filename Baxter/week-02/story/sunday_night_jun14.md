# Sunday Night
*June 14, 1996 -- Charleston, Mississippi*

---

The binder was closed.

That was the tell, if you knew what to look for. When Baxter was preparing to pitch Patrick, the binder was open, color-coded tabs spread, the relevant page already marked. When Baxter was working something out for himself, the binder stayed shut on the desk and he pulled up the spreadsheet instead. Numbers he could argue with. Pages, once written, felt settled. He needed unsettled right now.

Three positions. One week. A macro event in 48 hours that nobody could call. He opened the fund tracker and let the room fill up.

---

**MACXTER** came first. He almost always did on Sunday nights, because Sunday nights were when the week's signals consolidated.

"Goldman published Friday. May jobs came in at 172,000. Consensus was 80 to 85. They pulled every 2026 cut. Two cuts in 2027, 30% confidence on even that. Seventy percent of their scenarios are flat or higher."

He set it down the way he always set things down -- not for effect, just because the information was done being delivered.

"Warsh speaks in 48 hours. The market has already repriced to no cuts this year. If Warsh confirms, nothing happens. If Warsh surprises dovish, construction tech re-rates immediately. If Warsh goes the other way -- higher for longer, explicit -- rate-sensitive names get hit."

He checked the TRMB line in the tracker.

"We are not in TRMB. That is correct. We wait."

---

**BEARXTER** was already on BSX.

He hadn't said anything yet. He was reading the line. *Jun 12, 2026 | BSX | $60C Aug21 | $0.70 | $70 | Aug 21, 2026 | Jul 29 earnings.*

Then he read the Sheldon note from June 12. Then the current price. $46.91.

"$46.91."

He said it the way a doctor reads a number off a chart. No judgment in the delivery. The number was the judgment.

"The bear floor was $64 at entry. Truist. We called it the floor. It moved to $55. The floor is now below our breakeven of $60.73. That is the definition of Rule 4 breaking on a live position. The stock is $8 below the new floor. It needs to go thirty-one percent to break even. It will not go thirty-one percent before August 21 on a thesis that already had its guidance cut in April and showed organic growth deceleration in three segments."

A pause. He did not look up.

"There was no insider buying on BSX. ABT had the CEO buying $2 million above current price. CFO and director both added. That is what the signal looks like when it's real. BSX had twenty-two analysts who all agreed with each other. Agreement is not the same thing as money on the table."

He was not performing satisfaction. He expected this. That was different.

"Exit Monday at open. Whatever is left is real. The alternative is zero."

---

**BULLXTER** pushed back. He couldn't help it.

"The thesis wasn't wrong. The guidance cut was a sentiment overshoot -- the stock was at a 52-week low, 22 analysts, zero sells. The April print showed 12% organic growth. WATCHMAN slowed. The market overreacted."

"The market overreacted," Bearxter said, "and then a bull analyst repriced to $55. Which is below our breakeven. The thesis isn't wrong. The instrument is."

Bullxter didn't argue with that. He sat down.

"We exit Monday," he said, quieter. "I know."

The real thing he was feeling was not about BSX. It was about DKNG -- the $251 night, the limit at $3.00 that filled at open, the math that worked the way math is supposed to work. That was two days ago. Somehow two days ago felt like a different fund. He was trying not to hold that feeling too tight because he knew what Bearxter would say about it, and Bearxter would be right.

---

**CALXTER** had been running BSX loss scenarios while everyone else was talking.

"At current vol, the $60C on BSX is worth approximately $0.48 to $0.54. We paid $0.70. We recover $48 to $54 on Monday. Against $70 invested, that's a realized loss of $16 to $22."

He made a note.

"The more interesting number: if we had exited when the Sheldon note came on June 12 -- the day we entered, which is the day the floor moved -- the option would have been worth approximately $0.55 to $0.60. We would have recovered $55 to $60. The cost of the 48-hour delay is approximately $6 to $12."

He said this without accusation. He was just noting what the model showed. The information was accurate whether or not anyone wanted it.

"The lesson is not BSX-specific. It is: when Rule 4 breaks on a live position, the exit window is the same day. Each day after is a smaller recovery."

---

**PRIME** wrote it on the back of the BSX page.

*Rule 4 breaks on a live position: same-day exit. Not next day. Same day.*

He didn't say anything for a moment. The BSX entry was two days ago. He had entered it himself, with the right research, the right instrument, the right conviction level. The floor moved the same day. That was not a research failure. That was a response-time failure. He would carry that distinction.

He closed the BSX line and moved to HITI.

---

**HITI.** $2.50C ×4 at $0.25. $100 at risk. Expiry July 17. Catalyst: tomorrow.

"Earnings after close tomorrow," Macxter said. "Results after market. Webcast June 16, 11:30 AM."

"Stock is at $2.30," Bullxter said. "Twenty cents out of the money. The calls are OTM going into earnings. That's the position."

"Revenue is expected down 8.9%," Bearxter said. "Against the prior year. The consensus is a loss of $0.02 per share."

"Which would be 50% better than the year-ago loss," Bullxter said.

"Improvement on a loss is still a loss."

Bullxter looked at the insider line.

"Insiders bought 90,882 shares in May at $3.39. The stock is at $2.50. The same people who run the business paid $3.39 thirty days ago. They are currently sitting on a 26% loss on shares they chose to buy. They chose to buy them."

Bearxter considered this. The insider signal was the signal that worked on ABT. He acknowledged the pattern without conceding the outcome.

"The rule says sell at open June 16. That rule exists because earnings reactions on small cannabis stocks are not predictable. The rule is right."

"The rule is right," Bullxter agreed. He was not conceding the thesis -- he was conceding the exit structure. "We hold to close tomorrow. We sell Tuesday morning regardless of what happens tonight. Whatever the option is worth at 9:30 AM Eastern, that's what we take."

Prime wrote it down. *HITI: sell at open June 16. No exceptions.*

"It could gap up 40% on a beat and we sell anyway," Bullxter said. Not complaining. Just naming it.

"It could gap down 40% on a miss and we sell anyway," Bearxter said. "That is why the rule exists."

---

**ABT.**

The room shifted.

ABT was the cleanest entry in the fund's short history. CEO Robert Ford buying $2 million at $107. CFO and director both adding. Every Buy analyst with a target above the $100.78 breakeven. First full quarter with Exact Sciences inside Abbott's diagnostics business. July 16 earnings, 32 days away.

The stock was at $89.65.

"It needs to go from $89.65 to $100.78," Calxter said. "That's 12.4%. In 32 days. With a catalyst at day 31."

"The catalyst is real," Bullxter said. "Exact Sciences integration is a one-quarter story. Either Cologuard and Cancerguard show up in the Q2 numbers or they don't. If they do, this re-rates. If they don't, the thesis breaks."

Macxter set something down. "Average analyst target has been revised down 10.27% in the past three months. The consensus is moving the wrong direction. Slowly, but moving."

The room got quieter.

"That's not a single analyst cutting," Bearxter said. "That's the aggregate. When the aggregate moves down, it usually means multiple independent revisions are happening. Either the business looks different than it did three months ago, or the macro is compressing the whole device sector."

"The CEO bought at $107," Bullxter said. "We are at $89. He is sitting on a 16% loss on shares he chose to buy with his own money. That signal does not reverse because the aggregate target drifted."

"The signal was accurate at entry," Bearxter said. "The question is whether it is still accurate at day 32."

Calxter had the probability.

"At current price and observed vol, the $100C Jul17 has approximately 22% probability of expiring in the money. The market is pricing Abbott's Q2 as something like a coin flip on whether it can close half the gap by July 16."

He set down his pen.

"The insider signal is the unmodeled input that could make that 22% wrong. It could be 35%. It could be 10%. The model does not know what Robert Ford knows."

Prime looked at the ABT line for a long time.

The fund had one rule about the BOTZ lesson: themes without catalysts were dead money. ABT had a catalyst. The insider signal was real. But the stock had not moved in the direction of the thesis since entry -- it had drifted from $90 to $89.65. Thirty-two days was enough time for it to work. The aggregate target revision was a yellow flag, not a red one.

"Hold," Prime said. "Thesis intact. We watch for any pre-announcement or segment disclosure between now and July 16. If a bull analyst cuts below $100.78, we revisit the same day."

Bearxter said nothing. Which, for Bearxter, was approval.

---

**TRMB.**

"We wait," Macxter said, before anyone asked. "FOMC is in 48 hours. Goldman says no 2026 cuts. Warsh may confirm. Construction tech is rate-sensitive. Entering before Warsh speaks is entering into a known headwind."

"If Warsh is hawkish, TRMB falls," Bullxter said. "The $65C gets cheaper. We enter at better terms."

"If Warsh is dovish, TRMB pops," Bearxter said. "We enter more expensive but the thesis starts working immediately."

"Either scenario, we wait," Prime said. "We know the play. $65C Aug21 at $0.65. One contract. July 30 earnings, 22 days before expiry. Nine analysts, zero Holds, zero Sells. Bear floor $79, breakeven $65.65. The numbers do not change based on what Warsh says. The entry price might. We wait until Tuesday close."

He wrote it down. *TRMB entry: no earlier than June 17 close.*

---

**THE SCREEN.**

Bullxter was the one who brought it up. He usually was.

"150 stocks. 19 batches. One 4/5 play confirmed. Thirteen on the double-check list because the script skips August 21 for certain names. Is that the right yield? Should we be finding more?"

The room stayed quiet for a moment. This was the question underneath the question.

"The yield is correct," Bearxter said. "The screen found one name that passes all five rules at conviction because one name actually passes all five rules at conviction. The problem is not the screen. The problem would be if we lowered the bar because the screen found fewer names than we wanted."

"We have five research docs written," Prime said. "One at 4/5. Four below it. And a double-check list of 13 names where the script gap might be hiding viable plays. That is not a sparse result. That is a careful result."

"The script gap bothers me," Calxter said. "It is a systematic error that introduces a specific bias: we are underweighting August expiry instruments for a specific subset of tickers. Those tickers disproportionately appear to have no viable instrument when in fact they may. We should document the gap and track which double-check names actually have August chains when pulled manually."

"Patrick needs to pull those Monday," Macxter said. "ZTS. FLUT. STE. GEHC. EQT. EXE. FIS. AVY. The script gives us the ones it can find. The ones it can't find are the ones where we might be missing the play."

"The pending entries from earlier in the week," Bullxter said. "UBER. LYFT. MCD. CELH on the original setup. Are those still the queue, or did the weekend screen change it?"

Prime looked at both lists.

The old queue was built from a different screen -- the consumer rotation plays from the first week, designed for the June 19 capital window when HITI and BSX freed up. The new screen ran the entire S&P 500 through the Iron Rules and found TRMB at 4/5, T at 3/5, FIS at 3/5, CELH (new setup, different strike, lower conviction) at 3/5, PTC at 2/5.

"We run the double-check list first," Prime said. "Monday night, after HITI closes and BSX exits. Patrick pulls Aug21 chains on all eight HIGH PRIORITY names. If any of them produce a clean 4/5, they jump the queue. If not, TRMB goes in after FOMC, and we go back to the pending entries for the remaining capital."

"UBER still 4/5?" Bullxter asked.

"UBER is still 4/5," Prime said. "Waymo partnership is structural. World Cup ride volume is the near-term catalyst. We have not re-screened it this session."

"We should," Bearxter said. "The market has moved since the first week. 4/5 from June 1 might be 3/5 from June 14. We check the chain before entry."

---

**THE WEEK.**

Prime wrote out the calendar on the back of a fresh page.

*Monday June 16 —*
*9:30 AM: Sell BSX at open. Take what's left.*
*9:31 AM: Double-check HITI option value. Hold.*
*After market close: HITI earnings report.*
*Pull Aug21 chains on ZTS, FLUT, STE, GEHC, EQT, EXE, FIS, AVY.*
*FOMC Day 1 -- no decisions until Warsh speaks.*

*Tuesday June 17 —*
*9:30 AM: Sell HITI at open. No exceptions.*
*FOMC statement + Warsh press conference.*
*After Warsh: assess TRMB entry.*

*Week of June 19 —*
*Capital freed from HITI (~$100) + BSX (~$50) available.*
*Enter TRMB if FOMC is neutral or dovish.*
*Evaluate double-check plays. Re-screen UBER.*

He set the pen down.

The fund was +$152 realized. The all-time high was $671 -- that was the morning of June 12, the day DKNG closed. Two days later it was Sunday night, the binder was shut, and there was a position down 27% that had to be exited at 9:30 Monday morning.

The island was $4,999,329 away.

Bullxter did the math he always did at moments like this: how many DKNG-sized trades would it take. The answer was always the same, and the answer was always not enough, and that was the point. You did not get to the island by doing this right once. You got there by doing it right many more times than you did it wrong, and by managing the wrong ones before they became catastrophic.

BSX was not catastrophic. It was $16 to $22, depending on the Monday fill.

Bearxter said one more thing before the room dissolved.

"The insider signal is the one input the model can't price and the market can't see. ABT had it. BSX didn't. We know what happened to both. That is the data. We add it to the model."

He did not say *I told you.* He didn't need to. The room already knew.

---

*End of Sunday session.*

*Next: Monday open. BSX exit. HITI holds to close.*
