I have a second one.

Sturm, Ruger & Co. They make firearms. Pistols, revolvers, rifles, and shotguns. They have been doing this since 1949 in Southport, Connecticut, and they have never borrowed money to do it. Current long-term debt: zero. Cash and short-term investments on the balance sheet: $92.5 million. Current ratio: 3.9 to 1.

I will say that again. A manufacturing company that has been operating for 75 years with zero financial debt.

---

The business is cyclical and I want to be honest about that upfront.

Firearm demand in the United States runs on fear, politics, and election cycles. The 2020-2021 surge was one of the largest in the industry's history -- COVID, civil unrest, a presidential election, and then another -- and demand exploded. Ruger shipped 2.14 million units in 2021. Free cash flow that year: $143 million.

Then the fear subsided. Unit shipments fell to 1.37 million in 2023. Free cash flow that year: $18 million.

Dave does not buy a business at peak FCF. He also does not pass on a business because it had a trough year. The question is what the business looks like at mid-cycle, and what the floor looks like at the bottom.

At the bottom of the cycle, Ruger generated $18 million in free cash flow on a zero-debt balance sheet with $90+ million in cash. The lights stayed on. Nobody was diluted. No debt was taken on. The company waited for the cycle to turn, the same way it has waited through every prior trough since 1949.

In 2024, FCF recovered to $35 million. In 2025, $39 million. Unit volume is recovering and, more importantly, Ruger is taking market share -- their sell-through is outpacing total NICS background check volume. The trough appears to be behind them.

---

There is an ownership detail worth noting.

William B. Ruger Jr. -- son of the founder -- holds 26.79% of the company. Approximately $168 million at current prices. He does not run the business; professional management does. But he is sitting on $168 million of personal wealth tied to the health of this company and has no ability to liquidate it without moving the stock substantially. That is not the same as an active founder-operator, but it is not nothing.

The more interesting recent development: Beretta Holding -- the Italian firearms manufacturer -- acquired a 9.95% stake and staged a proxy fight. They lost, settled for two board seats, and are now represented in the boardroom. A strategic competitor buying in at or near the trough, accepting a below-threshold stake to get governance access, is a signal worth reading. They know the business. They bought it when it was out of favor.

---

The capital return history is the most Dave thing about this company.

When cash builds up and there is no productive use for it -- no debt to pay, no urgent capex -- Ruger sends it back. Special dividend of $5.00 per share in August 2020. Special dividend of $5.00 per share in January 2023. They have paid out over $100 million in dividends in individual years during demand peaks. The policy is approximately 40% of earnings each quarter, plus whatever accumulates in cash above what's needed to run the business.

This is not a company that hoards capital and deploys it on vanity acquisitions. When there is nothing better to do with money, they give it to the owners. That is the right answer.

---

The price is not right yet. At $39.30, mid-cycle normalized FCF puts this at approximately fair value on a 12-13x multiple. Dave needs 25% below intrinsic value to act.

Entry target: $34.00 to $35.00.

That is roughly where mid-cycle FCF at a 10x conservative multiple lands. It is also a price the stock has traded at before -- as recently as late 2024, before the partial recovery in unit volume moved it higher.

If the cycle softens, or if the broader market sells off, or if any of the legislative noise around firearms creates temporary fear in the stock price, this gets interesting. The business will be fine. It has been fine through every version of this story for seventy-five years.

Alert set at $35.50.
