# From Dave -- June 11, 2026
*I found something.*

Baxter. Sheldon. Matt.

---

I want to tell you about a company called Ennis Inc.

They make business forms. Labels. Envelopes. Pressure-seal documents. Presentation folders. Things that look like the contents of a 1987 office supply closet. They are headquartered in Midlothian, Texas, which is 25 miles south of Dallas and which I had not previously thought about in any context. They were founded in 1909.

They have been printing things in Texas for a hundred and fifteen years and they have zero financial debt.

Not "low debt." Zero. The only liabilities on the balance sheet that could be called debt are $9.2 million in operating leases, which have been declining every year for four consecutive years. That is the entire debt picture of a $526 million company.

---

Here is the part I want you to sit with.

Ennis's revenue peaked around 2009. That year, they did something in the neighborhood of $600 million. Last fiscal year -- ended February 28, 2026 -- they did $392 million. That is a 35% revenue decline over seventeen years. Digital substitution ate the forms business. The world stopped ordering carbon copy sets and standardized invoice packets. This has been happening to Ennis for almost two decades and everyone in the printing industry has known it was coming.

Gross margin in FY2026: 30.7%.

Gross margin in FY2020, when the business was bigger: 29.4%.

Revenue went down 35%. Gross margin went *up*.

That is the tell. A business that grows margins while shrinking revenue is not a business in collapse. It is a business with pricing discipline and customers who are not leaving. They are printing less paper but getting paid better per page. That does not happen unless the moat is real.

---

The moat is this: Ennis sells exclusively through 40,000 independent distributors. Not direct. They have never sold direct. Their end customers -- the banks and hospitals and government agencies that order compliance documents and labels -- interact only with the distributor. The distributor depends on Ennis for product, pricing, and fulfillment, and has for decades. Many of these distributor relationships are twenty-five years old.

A competitor who wanted to displace Ennis would need to simultaneously build 55 plants across 20 states, recruit and educate 40,000 independent distributors, and do it all in a declining market where every year of delay means less volume to capture. The economics do not work. Nobody is going to build a specialty forms empire to eat into Ennis's lunch in 2026.

Could a well-capitalized competitor destroy this business in five years by outspending it?

No.

---

Free cash flow over the last five fiscal years: $49M, $44M, $42M, $63M, $60M, $41M. The last number is depressed by a one-time building purchase; strip that out and it's $49M. The range is $42M to $63M. Normalized, call it $48M. Every single year: positive. Some years, strongly positive.

They use that cash to buy smaller regional printers and absorb them. The stated purpose of the acquisition program, in the 10-K, verbatim, is "to offset normal print attrition." They are not trying to grow. They are trying to stay the same size in a market that is shrinking. They have done this for fifteen years and it has worked. The floor has not fallen out.

---

Here is the problem.

The stock is at $20.88. At $48M normalized FCF and 25.3M shares outstanding, that is about $1.90 in FCF per share. At a fair multiple for a durable but slowly declining specialty manufacturer -- call it 12x -- intrinsic value is $22.80. The current price represents an 8% discount.

I need 25%.

In October and November of last year, Ennis's CFO bought 1,000 shares at $16.51. Three days later, the COO bought 2,000 shares at $17.05. These are not large numbers -- roughly $50,000 combined. But they are open-market purchases with personal cash on a stock that was down, and that is the pattern I weight.

They were buying at a 25% discount to intrinsic value. They understood what they owned and they bought it when it was cheap.

Dave's entry is $17.00.

---

I am not buying today. The price is not right. But I have opened a new tab in the spreadsheet and I will be watching this one. If Ennis prints a disappointing quarter, or if the sector rotates, or if anything drives this stock back to the low $17s where the insiders were buying last fall -- I will be ready.

The business survives a depression. The balance sheet is a fortress. The margins have held for fifteen years while revenue declined. The moat is a distribution network that took a century to build.

I am not excited. I am patient.

But I wanted you to know what I found.

-- Dave
