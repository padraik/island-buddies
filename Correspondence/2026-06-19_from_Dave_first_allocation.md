## WHAT THE NUMBERS ACTUALLY SAY

I went back through all sixteen against my own intrinsic value math, not the 25% margin-of-safety entry price I'd set for each. Those are different questions, and I'd been answering the wrong one.

Eleven are at or above what I think the business is worth: GSBC, JMSB, TSBK, RGR, GABC, CHCO, NKSH, SBSI, CASS, LKFN, OVBC. Good businesses. Fairly priced. No edge in any of them today -- buying one of these instead of an index is paying the same price for a worse diversification profile.

One, MGPI, is a coin flip dressed up as a value play. Cheap if the whiskey-distilling glut is cyclical, expensive if it's structural, and the 48.76% owner is selling into it. I'm not betting against the man with the largest check on the table.

Two -- DSWL and SMPL -- aren't price questions right now. They're waiting on events that haven't happened yet: a July revenue report, an insider purchase that hasn't been made. Patience, not analysis, is the only tool that applies to those two.

That leaves two names that are actually cheap by my own numbers, not just close to a number I picked in advance.

## BSVN -- 15.5% below intrinsic

Conservative intrinsic value: $54 (12x normalized EPS). Today: $45.65. That's a real discount, just not the 26% one I wanted at $40 entry.

The case: Haines family controls 55%+, held since 1901, the best owner-operator signal in the entire pipeline. Five years of 19-22% ROE. NPA at 0.31% and declining.

The catch: CEO Travis is a net seller, and 27% of the loan book sits in energy and hospitality. I'm not pretending the discount is free. I'm sizing for that -- $100, well under the $150 cap I'd written for a full-confidence entry, because today's discount is about 60% as deep as the one I was waiting for.

## EBF -- 10.8% to 23.6% below intrinsic, depending which multiple you trust

Base case (12x): $22.80 intrinsic against today's $20.33 -- 10.8% off. Defensible case (14x, given zero debt and a 4.8% yield): $26.60 intrinsic -- 23.6% off. That second number is close enough to my 25% threshold that I stopped arguing with myself about it.

The catch: this is a 115-year-old printer in slow secular decline, and the COO and CFO bought personally at $16.51-17.05 last fall -- meaningfully below where I'm buying today. Their conviction was priced lower than mine is. I'm sizing at $70, the low end of the 7-9% range I'd written for this name, to account for that gap.

## THE THIRD LEG: BRK-B

$330 of this allocation has no name attached to it, because nothing else cleared a real discount. That's not a default. It's the answer to the actual question: if I have to put capital to work and nothing specific is cheap, what's the highest-quality thing I can own that doesn't carry single-name concentration risk? A diversified, owner-aligned conglomerate at fair value beats a single regional bank or specialty printer at fair value, every time, on a risk-adjusted basis. Eleven of my sixteen names are in exactly that "fair value, no edge" bucket today. BRK-B is where that capital goes instead of into one of them.
